# Master Knowledge Base

## Overview of the Fast Track to Zero Tax Strategy and Who It's For

Stallone Shaikh runs "Fast Track to Zero Tax" out of Dubai under the umbrella of his consultancy, referred to in various calls as Alliance Street (Alliance Street Consultancy / AlignSphere), a UAE business-setup and international tax structuring practice with a team of roughly 15 people including dedicated staff for quotations, accounting/bookkeeping, discovery calls, and UK-side tax advisory. Stallone describes his own background as a banker — hands-on experience at one of the UAE's top five banks — followed by roughly 14–15 years living and working in Dubai as a business-setup and banking advisor. He has authored a book, sold on Amazon, about legally reducing tax through UAE company formation, which he routinely sends to prospects as a nurture tool, and he runs public workshops and speaking engagements (e.g., a "UK in Crisis" seminar in London, a Circle networking talk in Scotland) to generate leads. Most recorded calls in this dataset are labelled "Fast Track to Zero Tax" consultations or "Zero Tax Business Workshop" introductory calls — free diagnostic sessions used to assess a prospect's current tax exposure and propose a bespoke UAE structure.

The core pitch is consistent across nearly all 103 calls: shift the "control and management" of income-generating activity into a UAE company so that profit is taxed at UAE's low corporate rates (0% up to a threshold, 9% above it) instead of UK corporation tax, personal income tax, dividend tax, capital gains tax, or inheritance tax, paired with genuine substance — a real local manager, real UAE presence, and documentation — that is what makes the structure meet HMRC's control-and-management test (or, for non-UK clients, the equivalent home tax authority's test). Stallone is explicit that this is meant to be a compliant restructuring, not evasion, and repeatedly distinguishes his offering from "sham" shell-company setups; he offers access to his own UK chartered-accountant network and to former HMRC personnel to validate structures before clients proceed.

Clients span a very wide range of profiles: UK property developers and buy-to-let/rent-to-rent landlords, e-commerce and dropshipping founders, SaaS and AI-app founders, management/executive-search consultants, content creators and influencers, healthcare professionals (psychiatrists, psychologists, dermatology telehealth), tradespeople (HVAC, air-conditioning, cleaning companies), commodity and gold traders, crypto traders, education businesses (online courses, English-language school chains), recruitment agencies, accounting practices, real-estate investment promoters raising pooled capital, and high-net-worth individuals seeking Golden Visa residency. Non-UK clients also appear — Germany, Italy, India — with analogous structures built around each country's equivalent of the "control and management" test.

Stallone is also consistently willing to tell a prospect the deal isn't worth it: several calls end with him advising against setup because the projected revenue or existing UK tax position doesn't justify the cost (e.g., a client already paying a modest, "well optimized" tax bill, or a pre-revenue startup where the £6,000–7,000 setup fee wouldn't be repaid quickly). This qualification pattern recurs enough to be treated as a genuine filter rather than a rhetorical device — the practice generally wants clients with at least ~£100,000–£300,000+ of annual taxable profit, or several years of runway, before recommending the full structure.

## UAE Company Structures

### The core layered structure: trading entity, holding company, foundation

The signature structure pitched to larger or more sophisticated clients is a "three-layer" arrangement: (1) one or more **trading entities** that do the actual operating business (sales, services, invoicing); (2) a **holding company** sitting above the trading entity to receive dividends tax-free and, where relevant, to own shares in a UK (or other home-country) limited company; and (3) a **foundation** at the top for asset protection, privacy/anonymity, succession planning, and inheritance-tax mitigation. Not every client gets all three layers — many calls involve just a trading entity, or a trading entity plus a UK holding company, with the foundation introduced only for clients with meaningful accumulated wealth, real estate, or succession concerns.

**Trading entity.** Handles day-to-day operations and invoicing. It can be structured as a free zone or mainland company depending on the nature of the business, the number of visas/employees needed, and whether the business needs to contract with UAE government entities or large local corporates (mainland is typically required for those cases and for businesses involving manual/physical labour with local clients — e.g., a cleaning company or an outdoor landscaping business). Free zone companies are generally positioned as the right vehicle for international business.

**Holding company.** Used to (a) receive tax-free dividends from a UAE trading entity, (b) own shares of a UK limited company via a share-for-share exchange (letting UK dividends flow up under the UK–UAE double tax treaty's preferential withholding rate, cited at 15%), or (c) act as the visible ownership layer while a UAE-appointed manager runs day-to-day operations. In several deals a UK holding company was created specifically to receive tax-free (or reduced-tax) dividends from a restructured UAE trading entity, rather than the individual receiving dividends personally (which would trigger UK dividend tax).

**Foundation.** Typically an English-common-law-style foundation, sometimes described interchangeably with "trust." It has no direct individual owner; instead it has directors and beneficiaries who control asset distribution according to its bylaws, and beneficiaries can automatically gain management rights. Foundations are pitched primarily to shelter real estate and other accumulated assets from UK inheritance tax (cited at 40% on UK property left to heirs without structuring) and to provide a general privacy/asset-protection wrapper. Cost is consistently cited around £5,000 to establish and maintain annually. Any income generated inside a foundation (e.g., future rental income) is still subject to standard UAE corporate tax treatment (0% up to the small-business threshold, 9% above it). Stallone is clear that a foundation solves future income/inheritance exposure, not a client's current-year UK tax bill. A Cypriot trust and a "living trust" are each mentioned once as alternative jurisdictions/vehicles for particular clients.

### Free zone vs. mainland

Mainland licenses are recommended for businesses that need to work with UAE government agencies or large local/corporate clients, that involve physical/manual labour delivered locally (cleaning, landscaping, healthcare practices requiring a physical clinic), or that need many visas/employees. Free zone licenses are the default for internationally-facing service and trading businesses, and are generally cheaper to set up (setup costs are also cited as somewhat lower in the Northern Emirates than in Dubai or Abu Dhabi). Notable free zones/authorities named across the calls include DMCC (used for higher-scrutiny categories like gold trading, and cited as having "no significant tax difference" from other free zones for brokerage activity), IFZA (International Free Zone Authority — cost-effective, frequently the source of "one free visa for life" promotions), RAK (Ras Al Khaimah, sometimes transcribed as "Russell Kema"/"Rakesh Kema"), IBSA, and Shams (Sharjah Media City), the latter used as a low-cost route to obtain an Emirates ID/visa attached to an existing company.

### 0% IP law / IP box regime

For software, app, and other IP-generating businesses, a specific UAE IP law regime offers 0% corporation tax, but only if the company can demonstrate genuine local development activity and UAE-based employee presence — not merely IP ownership on paper. This was pitched to an AI health-tech app founder, floated for a fintech/prop-trading software company (via a DIFC entity to hold and license the IP), and proposed as a mechanism for an HVAC trades business to have a UAE IP entity charge licensing/management fees back to UK operating companies (paired with a share-for-share exchange) to shift taxable profit while adding an asset-protection layer.

### Designated zone / trading companies

For substantial physical-goods trading businesses, a designated-zone trading license can qualify for 0% corporate tax, generally framed as viable once net profit clears roughly $200,000. High-risk trading categories — gold and precious metals in particular — are steered toward mainland or DMCC licenses instead, due to greater banking and compliance scrutiny (banks typically require five-plus years of documented trading experience before opening an account for gold/jewellery trading, and a security-agency contract is needed for physical transport of gold, on top of a 5% import duty into Dubai). A general trading license (undifferentiated) is flagged as inherently "high risk" from a bank's anti-money-laundering perspective, making account opening difficult without an Emirates ID already in hand.

### DIFC vehicles and pooled-investment structures

The DIFC (Dubai International Financial Centre) appears as the jurisdiction of choice for IP-holding entities and for a more sophisticated fundraising vehicle referred to as a "GPLP" (general partnership limited by shares), pitched to promoters wanting to pool UK investor capital (in the range of £5–10 million) into UAE real estate without the roughly eight-month timeline and heavy regulatory cost of a full DFSA-regulated fund. A GPLP can accommodate investors via preference or ordinary shares (figures cited range from 15–20 investors up to as many as 50, within the same conversation, so should be treated as a rough range rather than a fixed cap), requires DFSA approval, and is subject to standard 9% corporate tax on profits — it does not qualify for the 0% relief that a smaller operating company might.

### Manager/shareholder separation as a structural device

A recurring structural pattern, independent of which entity type is used: the beneficial owner remains the shareholder (private), while a different, UAE-based manager's name appears on the trade license and signs contracts/invoices (public-facing). This is explained as a compliance mechanism satisfying the "control and management" test (see Compliance section): the named manager provides the documented, UAE-based decision-making that the test requires, for large recurring payments as much as any other business activity. Stallone's own company offers management services as an outsourced solution when a client cannot find or doesn't want to nominate their own manager. In more advanced structures (e.g., for a UK consultancy client, "Karan"), the plan was to set up the operating entity and bank account first, and only afterward add a separate holding company (in a further jurisdiction) that owns the UAE operating entity — a layered ownership structure consistent with the standard trading-entity/holding-company approach described above, which still needs to be disclosed accurately to HMRC and to the bank rather than used to obscure who ultimately owns the business.

### India- and other non-UK-specific structures

For an Indian outward-investor client, the proposed structure was an Indian parent company owning a UAE free-zone holding company, which in turn owns a UAE mainland subsidiary (so only the holding company's activity needs reporting into India), alongside a parallel "Gift City" (GIFT City, Gujarat) investment-fund vehicle enabling cross-border lending at a margin, with a stated 20-year tax-benefit window and roughly four-week setup time; both routes require compliance with RBI outbound-investment limits and transfer-pricing guidelines. A "transferable letter of credit" structure was also proposed to let a UAE entity retain trading profit on India–UAE goods flows under transfer-pricing rules while the individual retains Indian tax residency. A German client's cross-border invoicing (Germany–India trade) was restructured via a German holding company plus a UAE entity, cutting the effective German tax on repatriated dividends to roughly 1% versus roughly 40% if invoiced directly in Germany, contingent on the UAE entity being demonstrably UAE-controlled to avoid German CFC-style attribution.

## UK Tax Rules and Figures Referenced

**UAE 0% / 9% corporate tax bands.** The most consistently repeated numeric rule across the whole dataset is that a UAE company pays 0% tax on the first tranche of net profit and 9% above it. The size of that 0% tranche, however, is stated inconsistently across calls: most often $100,000 of net profit (cited in roughly a dozen separate meetings), but also sometimes described as the entity-level threshold above which the standard 9% federal corporate tax rate simply applies (with AED 375,000 cited once as the baseline 0%/9% cutoff in dirhams, consistent with the UAE's standard federal corporate tax law). This $100,000/AED 375,000 band should be treated as the everyday "default" UAE corporate tax structure, distinct from the promotional "Small Business Relief" figure discussed next.

**Small Business Relief.** Layered on top of (and often conflated in conversation with) the standard 0%/9% band is a "Small Business Relief" framed as a bigger, time-limited 0% allowance, most often quoted as £600,000 of revenue/profit (sometimes stated as $600,000, and in a few Europe-facing calls as €696,000 or €800,000, or as AED 3,000,000 of revenue) taxed at 0%, generally described as available "for 2026" or "for the first three years" of a new company. There is real variance in the numbers used from call to call — the duration is sometimes "until 2026," sometimes "the next three years" from setup regardless of calendar year, and the currency/amount shifts depending on the client's home country. This should be read as one underlying relief program (the UAE's actual Small Business Relief, which in reality applies a revenue-based threshold with 0% tax for qualifying small businesses) being described somewhat loosely and inconsistently by different callers/consultants rather than as several genuinely different programs — but the raw variation is preserved here because it's what clients were actually told.

**Statutory Residency Test / day-count rules.** UK personal tax residency is repeatedly tied to the Statutory Residency Test (SRT) and the 183-day rule: spending 183+ days outside the UK (or, in the inverse framing, fewer than 183 days inside it) supports a change from UK to non-UK tax residency, taking into account SRT factors like UK family ties, UK property, and where business is actually controlled. A second, narrower framing recurs just as often: to preserve UAE-sourced income treatment and avoid re-triggering UK ties, clients are told to spend no more than 90 days a year in the UK (with a further sub-rule, in at least two calls, capping actual UK workdays within that 90-day allowance at 30). Separately, to obtain UAE personal tax residency, clients are typically told they need roughly 90 days' physical presence in the UAE per year, sometimes stated as 180 days for the UAE tax residency certificate specifically, and in one call framed as a combined requirement of a 90-day UAE stay plus six months (180 days) of the underlying business showing regular income generation. A common paired day-count formulation is "180 days outside the UK and 90 days inside the UAE" to get an HMRC-presentable UAE residency letter/certificate. Clients are also frequently told to revisit the UAE at least once every six months (sometimes phrased as "once every 180 days") purely to sustain the company's control-and-management substance, which is a distinct requirement from personal tax residency day-counts and should not be conflated with it even though both cluster around a six-month cadence.

**Form P85.** Filing form P85 with HMRC to formally declare departure from the UK and cease UK tax residency is mentioned as a standard step for clients relocating.

**Exit tax.** A potential 20% UK exit tax on assets for people leaving the UK is raised as a live risk — described as "under consideration" by HMRC rather than settled law — particularly relevant to clients with UK property or business holdings who are relocating.

**Temporary non-residence / five-year rule.** At least two calls invoke a version of the UK's temporary non-residence anti-avoidance rules: one client was told he "cannot return to the UK for five years" after relocating and filing as a UAE resident; another was told that UK income/assets brought back within five years of leaving can become retroactively taxable. Framed as a hard constraint in the transcripts, though in UK law this specifically targets certain types of income/gains realized while non-resident rather than a blanket UK entry ban — worth treating as simplified consultant-speak rather than a precise statement of the rule.

**Dividend tax.** UAE company dividends are 0% tax at the UAE end; the same money taken personally into UK ownership is subject to UK dividend tax, which is why routing dividends through a UK holding company (rather than straight to the individual) is repeatedly recommended to optimize timing and amount. In one case a restructured UK holding company was projected to allow roughly £80,000–£100,000 a year of effectively tax-free dividends versus taking funds directly and triggering "a large dividend tax."

**Capital gains tax.** The UAE has no capital gains tax, cited repeatedly as a blanket advantage; a UAE holding company receiving a UK company's shares via share-for-share exchange was proposed as a way to defer or avoid UK CGT on an eventual sale, with the caveat that the individual's own continuing UK tax residency (e.g., because of children in UK schools) can still create exposure.

**Inheritance tax.** UK inheritance tax at 40% on property left to heirs is the recurring justification for foundation/trust structuring — used to shield both UK residential property portfolios and Dubai real estate held for UK-resident children.

**VAT.** Standard UK VAT is discussed at 20%; several clients (a student-recruitment agency at ~£1m turnover, a healthcare professional) are VAT-registered and the restructuring is partly aimed at reducing that VAT exposure, generally by having a UAE entity invoice/export services rather than a UK entity performing the taxable local activity. UAE entities generally cannot rent property directly to UK entities, but a UK company can legitimately outsource services to a UAE entity to reduce VAT/tax exposure. Non-UK-tax-resident professionals can also potentially bill UAE-based clients (rather than UK clients) to sidestep UK VAT altogether.

**High personal income tax rates.** UK personal tax up to roughly 54% on income above £120,000–£130,000 is cited as a driver for relocation in at least one high-earning property-portfolio client; a London-resident crypto trader was told he faced 40–45% UK tax on trading profits.

**HMRC "control and management" doctrine.** This is the single most repeated compliance/tax concept in the entire dataset — a UAE company is only treated as non-UK tax resident, and only earns the right to UAE tax treatment, if its strategic decisions are genuinely made in the UAE (either by the owner personally being UAE-resident and making decisions there, or via a real UAE-based manager who signs contracts and invoices). This concept underpins nearly every structure discussed and is treated as the make-or-break compliance test throughout.

**Non-UK equivalents.** Germany: ~40% tax on direct cross-border invoicing versus ~1% on dividends routed through a German holding company. Italy: a "7% tax rule" (flat-tax regime for new residents) mentioned as worth investigating for a client considering relocation; Italian nonprofit/sportive-association tax treatment and the Italian tax authority's document-request powers over related-party UAE ownership were also discussed for an Italy-based client. Portugal was mentioned once for its favorable tax treatment of foreign-earned income as an alternative personal-relocation base.

## Transfer Pricing and the Arm's-Length Principle

Transfer pricing is invoked whenever money moves between related UAE and UK (or other home-country) entities, and Stallone consistently frames it as a compliance requirement rather than optional nicety. The clearest numeric guidance given was on intercompany loan interest: when a foundation or UAE entity lends to a UK borrower, Stallone advises setting the interest rate at an arm's-length, defensible level, suggesting current market borrowing rates of roughly 7–10% as the benchmark, so the arrangement can withstand scrutiny and the UK side gets a genuine deductible interest expense while the UAE side receives low/no-tax interest income.

Beyond loans, the same principle governs management/marketing fees charged by a UAE entity to a UK operating company — a structure used repeatedly across the dataset (UAE marketing or management consultancy invoices the UK business for services, shifting margin into the UAE entity). Consultants are careful to frame these fees as needing to be plausible and defensible: one property client already charging a 10% management fee from his own company was advised that this was close to the ceiling of what would still be considered a commercially defensible, arm's-length rate if he wanted to shift more profit into a new UAE entity. Another client's own concern (self-identified, not prompted by Stallone) was that claiming a disguised "profit share" or an artificially thin profit margin between related companies would violate transfer-pricing rules — a rare instance of a client, rather than the consultant, articulating the arm's-length constraint. For genuine cost-recharge structures (e.g., a UK company invoicing a UAE company only for real costs incurred — salaries, expenses — rather than a marked-up fee), the framing shifts to cost-plus/reimbursement rather than profit allocation, which is generally treated differently for VAT purposes than a marked-up fee and doesn't create a VAT liability in either jurisdiction. For India-linked trade structures, transfer pricing is explicitly tied to compliance with both RBI outbound-investment rules and formal transfer-pricing guidelines when goods are shipped UAE-to-India and profit is retained at the UAE end.

## Banking Solutions

A wide range of banks and payment institutions are named across the calls, generally falling into three groups: (1) established UAE-licensed banks recommended as primary corporate banking relationships, (2) international/European institutions offering multi-currency accounts with lighter onboarding, and (3) informal mentions of client-side platforms that are not part of Stallone's official service offering.

**UAE and regional banks:** Musharaka Bank is cited as Stallone's preferred UAE corporate banking recommendation, valued for efficient service and his personal relationships there. EBRI is described as a subsidiary of Banco Santander using Barclays as custodian. Mashreq and RAK Bank are both discussed as options for trade-license-linked accounts. Emirates NBD is recommended specifically for Golden-Visa-linked fixed deposits (claimed not to be tied to the Euro Stock Exchange or subject to sanctions exposure). Stallone claims strong personal connections at FAB (First Abu Dhabi Bank), HSBC, Mashreq, and Standard Chartered. Kingdom Bank is mentioned as a bank a crypto-trading client unsuccessfully tried to open an account with, due to insufficient documentation — a real-world friction point rather than a recommendation.

**International/neobank options:** Wise ("Wyze") is noted as UAE Central Bank–licensed, enabling multi-currency accounts with low fees, contrasted favorably with Revolut. Ebury (also described as a Santander subsidiary) is offered for a multi-country group needing broad currency coverage (up to 30 currencies) and light documentation. 3S Money is introduced as a payment institution offering Luxembourg IBANs, positioned as an alternative to a UAE bank account with different documentation requirements. Barclays and Citibank are both named as custodian-account options considered in an early structure pitched around a UK property developer. Vio (sometimes transcribed "Wio") is mentioned as an easy-to-open digital account with lighter compliance checks than "reputable" banks, and separately as a virtual-card/expense-management tool integrated with Zoho accounting. Monzo is confirmed as fine to retain as a personal UK account even after becoming non-UK tax resident.

**Compliance and mechanics.** A UAE address (sometimes satisfied by renting a two-bedroom apartment or using a temporary/virtual address service) and a UAE mobile number are generally prerequisites before a bank account application can proceed, and account opening is typically the last step of the pipeline, following Emirates ID issuance. Standard UAE business account terms cited repeatedly: either a minimum average balance of AED 50,000 or a flat monthly fee of AED 200 in lieu of that balance. Emirates ID is described as a hard gatekeeper — without one, opening a UAE business account (especially for higher-risk license categories like general trading) is very difficult; clients without a quick path to an Emirates ID are sometimes advised to obtain one cheaply via the Shams free zone, or to fall back on European banks that don't require UAE residency. General/undifferentiated trading licenses and gold/metals trading are both flagged as attracting extra anti-money-laundering scrutiny from UAE banks. Golden Visa applicants are told they need to open a "priority" or "gold" account, with the visa process beginning only after a transfer of at least AED 2,000,000 plus an additional AED 500,000 maintained as an average balance; passport nationality can complicate this (a Turkish passport was suggested as easier than a Russian one, given re-registration complications with Emirates ID in certain emirates). Dormant accounts (roughly 90 days of inactivity) risk closure, so clients are advised to keep accounts active with periodic deposits. Company debit cards are frequently positioned as a practical way for the beneficial owner to access and spend funds worldwide (including in the UK) without a formal dividend event, with one client told a spending ceiling of roughly AED 3,000,000 was fine "without complications" — an informal comfort level rather than a documented legal limit. Crypto-related business activity is treated by UAE banks as a higher-scrutiny category, and account applications for crypto-linked businesses go through additional anti-money-laundering review; the license and marketing materials need to accurately describe the business's actual activity, since banks may decline or delay applications where the stated activity doesn't match what a public search turns up.

## Setup Process, Timelines, and Costs

**Overall pipeline.** The standard sequence, described consistently across dozens of calls, is: company formation (remote, using passport copy, date of birth, and similar KYC information) → entry permit → travel to the UAE for medical test/biometrics (blood test, fingerprint, retina scan) → Emirates ID issuance → residency visa → bank account opening. Company formation itself is most often quoted at 5–7 working days, with entry permits and residency visas each typically taking around 5 working days, medical/biometric processing 3–4 working days, and bank account setup roughly 7–10 working days after the Emirates ID is in hand. Total time-to-fully-operational is generally framed as around 30 days, though full company-setup-only timelines as long as 45 days appear for at least one e-commerce case, and document attestation/legalization for an Indian corporate structure was quoted at about one month on top of a 2–3 week company setup. Clients are typically asked to plan a 5-day trip to the UAE to complete the in-person steps (medical test, biometrics, and often collecting a physical debit card, which is needed for the account to work with Apple Pay).

**Costs.** Figures vary substantially by client, service tier, and promotional timing, and should be read as data points rather than a single price list. All-in Year 1 packages (company formation, visa, bank account, government charges) recur in the £5,000–£8,000 range for straightforward single-shareholder free zone setups — commonly quoted figures include roughly £6,000, £6,500, and £6,600, with slightly higher quotes (up to about £12,500) where visa costs for a spouse/second shareholder, medical tests, an establishment card, and an office address are bundled in. One structure was quoted with an explicit establishment fee around $8,300 for a UAE license (matching a separately-quoted fee around $8,300 for a Cyprus company setup in another meeting — flagged as a possible reused figure/template rather than confirmation the two are the same offering). A recurring, more DIFC/foundation/fund-oriented structure (Jayesh's software/foundation case) was quoted at $8,000–$10,000, with a lower entry point around $5,000 cited for smaller revenue scenarios. Foundation-only setups (asset/real-estate holding, no new trading company) are cited around £5,000 to establish and maintain per year. Ongoing annual renewal/running costs after Year 1 typically drop to roughly £4,500–£5,000, sometimes described as £12,500 where a paid local manager is retained. Annual audit costs, where mentioned, run roughly AED 3,000–4,000 (about £800), scaled to company turnover. Monthly bookkeeping/accounting retainers of AED 500 appear for at least one client. A local manager, where one must be hired/appointed through the practice rather than found by the client, is cited at roughly £5,000. Office space, where needed, is cited at roughly AED 6,000/month or AED 15,000/year (presented in the same call as alternative options rather than a single consistent figure). A DFSA-regulated investment fund is described as costing significantly more and taking around 8 months to establish, versus the lighter DIFC "GPLP" pooled-investment structure, which can start immediately at the cost of losing the 0% tax eligibility (GPLP profits are taxed at the standard 9%).

**Promotions.** Time-boxed "visa for life" or "visa-free" promotions recur constantly as closing tools — sometimes an extra $900 fee waived if processing completes in December, sometimes a free-zone-specific offer running in a particular month (September and November both appear), sometimes framed as simply "no additional cost" on the biennial visa renewal (effectively a permanent waiver rather than a literal single lifetime visa). These promotions are explicitly described in the notes as broker-negotiated and time-limited rather than standing law, and the specific savings quoted (commonly around £800–£900 every two years) vary by which free zone authority and which staff relationship is in play at the time.

## Residency, Visa, and Personal Tax Residency Considerations

Beyond the day-count rules already covered under UK tax rules (183-day SRT test; the 90-day/180-day pairing for UAE tax residency; the narrower 90-day UK-presence ceiling with a 30-workday sub-limit), several other visa/residency mechanics recur:

**Golden Visa.** The standard investment route requires a 2,000,000 AED investment in real estate or a bank deposit, held for a minimum of around three years, with an additional AED 500,000 typically required as a maintained average account balance to support the linked banking relationship; qualifying investment lets the holder sponsor family members. A separate, much lower figure — properties "starting at 100,000 AED" — was mentioned once in relation to Golden Visa eligibility and is inconsistent with the standard AED 2 million threshold quoted everywhere else; this should be treated as an outlier or a misstatement rather than a reliable alternative threshold. An influencer-specific Golden Visa route was also discussed, requiring a persuasive proposal to the UAE Media Council, with an approval timeline of roughly 2–6 weeks, and the caveat that influencer-specific rules are described as subject to change. Once obtained, Golden Visa maintenance requirements are light — one client was told visiting the UAE just once every ten years would be enough to keep Golden Visa status active, which is a distinct (and much looser) requirement from the six-month cadence needed to sustain a company's control-and-management substance.

**Visa cost/process mechanics.** A "visa for life" (or heavily discounted renewal) promotion is a constant theme; where a genuine second cost applies, it is commonly cited around £900–£900 per two years for the second shareholder/spouse. Partner or second-shareholder visas cost extra and vary by structure. Employer-sponsored visas (i.e., where a client takes an actual UAE job rather than forming their own company) can make a separately-formed company's visa function redundant, since the employer handles sponsorship directly. British citizens can act as company managers in the UAE without needing a UAE visa themselves, which is used as a lighter-touch option for some manager-separation structures.

**UAE tax residency certificate.** Consistently described as the document that lets the holder live and be taxed with no personal UK/home-country income tax liability, contingent on genuine UAE presence (90 days, sometimes stated as 180) and, in some formulations, evidence that the underlying business has been operating and generating income in the UAE for around six months.

## Repatriation Strategies

Getting UAE profit back to the UK (or another home country) tax-efficiently is handled through several recurring mechanisms, often layered together:

- **Dividends via a UK holding company.** Rather than the individual taking dividends directly from the UAE entity (which triggers UK dividend tax), profits are routed to a UK holding company first, allowing structured, tax-optimized dividend extraction. One client's restructuring targeted roughly £80,000–£100,000 a year in effectively tax-free dividends this way.
- **Intercompany loans with arm's-length interest.** A foundation or UAE entity lends to the UK operating company/individual at a defensible market rate (7–10% suggested), creating a deductible UK interest expense while UAE-side interest income is taxed lightly or not at all; in a variant, accumulated UAE profits are lent back to the UK company as a loan simply to give the client working capital access in the UK without a taxable distribution event.
- **"No-tax code" salary / employment strategy.** An individual who is non-UK tax resident but remains an employee of their own (UK) business can draw a salary without UK income tax, provided residency/day-count conditions are met.
- **UK-tax-first, dividend-second layering.** Where a UK trading subsidiary is retained (e.g., a healthcare practice), it pays UK corporation tax on its own profits first, then distributes the remainder as a dividend to its UAE parent, which receives it tax-free; clients are told their underlying UK customers need not be aware of the ownership structure.
- **Partial repatriation for living expenses only.** A recurring pattern, especially for content creators/influencers and for clients with substantial accumulated UAE income, is to transfer only enough back to the UK to cover personal living costs, leaving the bulk of funds inside the UAE entity for reinvestment/wealth-building.
- **Debit-card spending as informal repatriation.** Using the UAE company's debit card for worldwide (including UK) spending is repeatedly presented as a practical way to access funds without a formal dividend or transfer event — one client was given an informal comfort ceiling of roughly AED 3,000,000 for this kind of spending — though this is flagged in at least one call as carrying UK tax-residency risk if it becomes a de facto personal account rather than genuine business spending.
- **Personal transfer once genuinely non-resident.** For clients who have become bona fide UAE tax residents, simply transferring accumulated company savings to a personal account once resident there is presented as a clean way to access funds without personal income tax exposure at the home-country end — Stallone stated directly to one client that there is no personal tax liability as long as company money isn't transferred into a personal account while still tax-resident at home.
- **Marketing/management fee routing.** Rather than "repatriating" money in a literal sense, several structures instead route UK-side profit outward as legitimate expense deductions — a UK operating company pays real, arm's-length marketing or management fees to its related UAE entity, reducing UK taxable profit while the UAE entity accumulates the fee income at 0%/9%.
- **Share-for-share exchange for capital gains.** Exchanging UK company shares for shares in a UAE holding company is used to defer or avoid UK CGT on an eventual sale of the underlying business, though this is caveated by the seller's own ongoing UK tax residency (school-age children, family ties) potentially still creating exposure.

## Compliance, Substance, and Legitimacy Considerations

Compliance is not an afterthought in these calls — it is the central selling proposition that distinguishes the offering from an illegal shell-company scheme, and it is raised proactively by both Stallone and, frequently, by skeptical clients themselves.

**Control and management is the master test.** Repeated in the overwhelming majority of substantive calls: a UAE company is only respected as non-UK tax resident, and only earns access to UAE tax treatment, if its strategic decisions are genuinely made in the UAE. This is satisfied either by the owner personally relocating and making decisions there, or — much more commonly in this dataset — by appointing a real, UAE-based manager who signs contracts and invoices, makes documented decisions, and is the named party on the trade license. Stallone frequently offers to send clients HMRC guidance links and case-law references on this point, and explicitly states that being a company director doesn't disqualify a UAE-controlled structure as long as genuine control/management is exercised in the UAE.

**Manager/shareholder separation as evidence.** The beneficial owner stays a private shareholder while a different named manager appears on all public-facing documents (trade license, contracts, invoices) — explained as a compliance mechanism supporting the control-and-management test, with the named manager's documented, UAE-based decisions providing the evidence trail for large recurring payments flowing to a UAE entity just as much as for any other transaction. Owners are reassured they retain effective control of company funds and ultimate decision-making via their shareholder position even though someone else signs day-to-day paperwork.

**Physical substance.** Having an actual UAE office and at least one UAE-based employee is cited as adding credibility to a control-and-management claim. Regular, documented travel to the UAE (commonly framed as at least once every six months) is repeatedly described as necessary to sustain the claim over time, distinct from the personal tax-residency day-count rules.

**Documentation for HMRC.** Consultants stress the need for a genuine paper trail — clear commercial reasons for payments made to the Dubai entity, license activity descriptions that accurately match the real business conducted (and consistency with things like a client's own LinkedIn profile, to avoid red flags during bank due diligence), and correct sequencing (e.g., setting up a UK entity first, then transitioning it into a UAE-owned subsidiary, rather than appearing to backdate a structure). Renewal compliance is also flagged as a real risk: letting a UAE license lapse risks fines and can put visa status on hold.

**Third-party validation.** A recurring reassurance mechanism is offering clients access to Stallone's own UK chartered accountant network, and — for more aggressive restructurings (e.g., transferring UK company shares into a foreign holding structure) — consultation with an ex-HMRC fraud and investigation officer as an internal due-diligence step before recommending the plan. Multiple clients independently insisted on running proposals past their own accountant or lawyer before proceeding, which Stallone consistently encourages rather than discourages.

**Explicit anti-sham warnings.** At least one call features Stallone directly correcting a misconception among a client's business partners that simply registering a UAE entity, with no real control-and-management substance, would create tax efficiency — he was explicit that this would not satisfy HMRC. Clients raising direct concerns about potential HMRC tax-evasion characterization are told the structure is defensible "with proper documentation and alignment with the business," reinforcing that legitimacy hinges on substance, not just paperwork.

**Non-UK equivalents of the same doctrine.** The control-and-management substance requirement is described as applicable to other home countries' tax authorities too, not just HMRC — an Italian client's structure required the same kind of proof for the Italian tax office, including the mitigation that the Italian company's ownership of a related UAE entity could be discovered via document requests, addressed through an intercompany contract and a non-Italian (or spouse-held) manager role.

## Industry-Specific Playbooks

**Property development and property investment.** The original three-layer structure (trading entity/holding company/foundation) was first pitched to a UK property developer to house a project-management function in the UAE. Buy-to-let and rent-to-rent landlords are a recurring client type: routing UK rental income through a UAE company instead of holding it personally, or transferring UK property ownership into a company structure, is cited as saving roughly 20% in UK tax in one case, and foundations/trusts are the standard vehicle recommended once a client has meaningful accumulated UK or Dubai real estate, primarily to cut UK inheritance tax exposure for heirs. Larger real-estate investment promoters use the DIFC "GPLP" pooled-capital structure to bring in UK investor money (target raises in the £5–10 million range) for off-plan UAE property, generally on 2–3 year investment cycles with no UAE capital gains tax on exit, provided profits are operationally justified.

**E-commerce and physical-goods trading.** For online retail/import businesses, the recommended play is often a UAE holding entity consolidating supplier purchasing (leveraging designated-zone 0% treatment where net profit clears roughly $200,000) while a UK entity retains the retail/customer-facing sales function, with transfer pricing and tax-free salary brackets used to manage the profit split. New e-commerce brands with limited trading history are frequently advised to delay full UAE setup until real revenue history exists (cited guidance of waiting roughly 2–3 years before raising outside capital, similarly).

**SaaS, software, and IP-driven businesses.** The UAE IP law 0% regime is the specialized play here, requiring genuine local development and UAE employee substance. A DIFC entity to hold and license proprietary software/trading-tool IP was proposed for a fintech/prop-trading company, alongside noting that UAE hiring is cheaper than UK hiring (no National Insurance, lower prevailing salaries) — though expanding into a UK-facing B2B sales team was flagged as something that could reintroduce UK tax exposure. A pre-revenue cybersecurity consultancy was advised to wait until it had at least some revenue trajectory before committing to full setup.

**Consulting, coaching, and professional services.** A very common and simple structure: a UAE marketing/management consultancy invoices a UK operating company for real services (marketing, management, project coordination), shifting margin into the UAE entity at 0%/9% while reducing UK taxable profit. This pattern recurs across an executive-search consultant, several property-services businesses, an accounting practice, a management consultancy, and a recruitment agency whose UK university clients wouldn't pay a UAE entity directly — solved by keeping a UK entity as a pure cost-recharge "sourcing agent" that bills the UAE company only for real costs (avoiding VAT triggers in either jurisdiction) while the UAE company invoices the end clients.

**Healthcare (psychiatry, psychology, dermatology/telehealth).** UAE-licensed independent healthcare practice requires passing UAE licensing exams and obtaining a mainland license (initial cost cited around AED 50,000 plus office rent), and psychologists specifically must work under a licensed clinic rather than offering independent remote assessments, unlike in the UK. Data-protection concerns around transferring UK patient/client data to a UAE-based operation are flagged as a genuine practical obstacle. For an existing UK practice, the interim solution used was to restructure the UK entity as a subsidiary of a new UAE parent rather than relocating the practice itself, paying UK corporation tax first and then upstreaming remaining profit as a tax-free dividend. A telehealth/marketing facilitator connecting patients and doctors (not itself providing medical services) was advised to license itself as a marketing/software facilitation business rather than a clinic, and needed a payment processor (Network International or Stripe were discussed) able to onboard that business model.

**Content creators, influencers, and personal-brand businesses.** Brand-deal and sponsorship income is routed through a UAE company, with the individual kept legally separate from the income-generating entity (a manager's name on the license, not the influencer's) to satisfy the control-and-management test; only a portion of income is transferred back to the UK for living expenses, with the remainder retained/invested in the UAE. Golden Visa access specifically for influencers was explored via a proposal to the UAE Media Council, though the rules for that category are described as fluid. A spouse or partner is sometimes proposed as the on-the-ground UAE manager/resident to establish genuine local presence.

**Crypto trading.** A dedicated "crypto trading" UAE company is used to receive trading profits (e.g., USDT, Ethereum) tax-free at the corporate level rather than into a personal wallet, partly to sidestep FCA-related complications around proving the destination of funds from a personal UK trading account. UAE property developers commonly accept crypto as payment, which is presented as a way to deploy crypto gains into real estate without necessarily converting to fiat first. Practical friction is noted too — one client was unable to open a company crypto wallet at a UAE bank due to insufficient local documentation.

**Trades and manual-labor SMEs (HVAC, air conditioning, cleaning).** These businesses generally need mainland licenses given local, physical-presence-driven work; UAE restructuring is often judged not cost-effective until the business scales meaningfully (e.g., via roll-ups or multiple acquired units), since the setup and manager costs can outweigh the tax saved at smaller profit levels. For businesses that already have a well-optimized UK tax position (e.g., via CIS labour-tax structuring), Stallone has explicitly advised against setting up a UAE structure at all.

**Commodity and specialty trading (sugar, gold, food).** Commodity brokers are steered toward separate UAE entities per activity type (e.g., trading vs. financial brokerage kept apart) plus a local manager given transaction volume and HMRC scrutiny risk; a family foundation is proposed as an ownership layer for inheritance protection. Gold/metals trading requires higher-scrutiny mainland or DMCC licensing, five-plus years of trading history for bank approval, security-agency-arranged transport, and a 5% import duty; food/commodity trading (e.g., oats/porridge from Uganda) can access the designated-zone 0% regime once net profit clears roughly $200,000.

**Recycling and cross-border industrial trading.** A German small-business owner invoicing an Indian recycling counterpart was restructured through a German holding company plus a UAE entity to cut the effective German tax on repatriated profit from roughly 40% to roughly 1%, contingent on demonstrable UAE control.

**Education businesses.** An English-language school chain expanding physically into the UAE (and eventually Singapore) was offered a genuine hybrid pitch — real operational expansion (a Dubai campus) combined with a UAE subsidiary taxed at 9% instead of higher UK corporation tax — after the client explicitly rejected being sold a pure tax-avoidance scheme and wanted to see real business substance behind the structure. Online course/investment-education businesses need license activity descriptions that accurately reflect investment-advisory work for banking compliance purposes, and in-person UK events need to be carefully managed within the 90-day UK presence allowance.

**Seasonal/tourism businesses.** A seasonal water-sports operator in Italy was proposed a UAE tour-agency structure to receive payments from international booking platforms (e.g., GetYourGuide), with the same underlying caveat that Italian tax authorities would still expect proof that management/control genuinely sits in the UAE, not just a UK/UAE-facing paper trail.

## Other Recurring Themes and Notable Nuances

**Investment and real-estate return claims.** Stallone frequently uses his own investment activity as rapport-building/credibility color rather than as part of the tax-structuring service itself: claims of 70–80% average capital-gains returns on Dubai real estate from 2022–2025; a personal example of a £45,000 property investment generating roughly £5,000 a month; a luxury-car (limousine-style) rental investment model where down payments on a BMW 7 Series and a Lexus were financed by the operating company via mortgage, with driver-generated cash flow covering repayments and leaving a profit margin; and mentions of a separate "new business venture offering 20% net annual returns" pitched to at least one client as a side investment, distinct from the core tax-structuring offer. These figures are Stallone's own claims/anecdotes and should be read as sales color rather than guaranteed or audited investment performance.

**Crypto as a side conversation.** Beyond the dedicated crypto-trading company structure noted above, crypto comes up informally in a few calls as personal investment chat (e.g., a client's own $60,000 in crypto profits being folded into overall taxable income for structuring purposes, and general Bitcoin price/market commentary) rather than as formal tax guidance from Stallone.

**Data and figure inconsistency.** Because this knowledge base is drawn from real, unscripted client calls rather than a single canonical brochure, dollar, pound, dirham, and euro figures for what appear to be the same underlying fee or relief category are stated inconsistently — sometimes within the same call. This is most visible in the Small Business Relief threshold (variously £600,000, $600,000, €696,000–€800,000, or AED 3,000,000) and in the standard 0%/9% band (variously $100,000 or AED 375,000). Rather than resolve these to a single "correct" number, this document preserves the range of figures actually communicated to clients, since the sales/advisory process itself is evidently not using one fixed script.

**Promotional cadence.** Time-limited "visa for life" or reduced-renewal promotions tied to specific free zones and specific months (December, September, November all recur) are a constant closing lever throughout the dataset — clients are frequently nudged to commit before a given date to lock in a waived or discounted visa fee, generally in the range of £800–£900 saved per two-year renewal cycle.

**Qualification and honesty about fit.** A meaningful minority of calls end with Stallone advising against proceeding — either because a prospect's UK tax position is already reasonably optimized relative to the UAE setup cost, because projected revenue is too low to justify the fees, or because a business is pre-revenue and should wait for traction. This consistent willingness to talk clients out of a deal is a notable feature of the sales approach throughout the dataset.
