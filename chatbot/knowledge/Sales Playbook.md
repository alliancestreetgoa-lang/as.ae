# Sales Playbook

This playbook is reverse-engineered from 103 real client call transcripts run by Stallone Shaikh (Alliance Street Consultancy / Fast Track to Zero Tax). It documents the recurring patterns in how Stallone actually opens, qualifies, pitches, handles pushback, and closes — grounded in what happened across dozens of calls, not a single anecdote. Use it as a study guide for how a new consultant should run these calls.

## 1. Call Opening / Rapport Building

Stallone rarely opens cold on tax. Across the transcripts, a clear minority but consistent pattern of calls (roughly 1 in 6-8) open with a few minutes of pure personal small talk before any business is mentioned — weather ("a grey and wet day in Brighton" vs. "a tornado in Dubai"), travel plans, or what each person is doing that week. This isn't wasted time — it's a deliberate warm-up that establishes he's a real relationship-driven person, not a script-reader, before he pivots into business.

The more universal opening move, seen across nearly every call, is **self-positioning before pitching**. He introduces himself (or is reintroduced) with a specific, credibility-bearing frame rather than a generic sales title, e.g.:

> "Stallone introduced himself as the founder of Alliance Street Consultancy, which helps UK business owners structure their businesses in the UAE while ensuring HMRC compliance."

This "founder + compliance-first" framing does two things at once: it signals authority (he's not a reseller, he built the firm) and it pre-empts the "is this even legal?" objection before it's asked, by putting HMRC compliance in the same sentence as the pitch.

Where the prospect is a referral (a very common source — see Section 6), he name-drops the referrer early ("Sanjeeb referred Zaid to him") to borrow trust immediately. Where the prospect came from a workshop or webinar ("Free Zero Tax Business Workshop" calls make up a large chunk of the dataset), he treats the opening as a continuation of a conversation the prospect already started, moving quickly to "tell me about your situation" rather than re-explaining who he is.

**Pattern for new reps:** spend the first 2-3 minutes on genuine, situational rapport (their week, their location, how they found him/the workshop), then transition with a one-line credibility statement that bundles "who I am" + "what I do" + "and I keep you compliant" into a single sentence, before asking your first discovery question.

## 2. Discovery / Qualifying Questions

Discovery is the largest and most consistent part of every call. Stallone almost never pitches a structure before he has pulled hard numbers out of the prospect. The recurring question categories, seen across dozens of calls, are:

**A. Income / turnover (asked on nearly every call — "turnover" appears 16+ times across transcripts)**
- "What's your turnover?" / "What's the business doing in revenue?"
- Follow-up: "How much of that is profit, and how much are you currently paying in tax?" (he gets prospects to state their current UK tax bill out loud — e.g. Farhan: "approximately £8-9,000 in yearly taxes," Craig: "£37,000-38,000 in taxes annually" — this number becomes the anchor he later contrasts against the UAE savings)

**B. Current structure and advisors**
- "Do you have a UK limited company already?" / "Is it a sole trader or a limited company?"
- "Who's your current accountant, and how do they feel about this kind of thing?" (asked at least 7 times — he's probing whether the prospect's existing advisor will be an obstacle, and often offers to introduce a more "open-minded" UK accounting firm if the current one seems resistant)

**C. Physical presence / residency status (the single most load-bearing discovery question, because the entire pitch hinges on it)**
- "How many days a year do you actually spend in the UK?"
- "Where do you currently live, and where's your family based?"
- This maps directly onto the UK Statutory Residency Test (raised explicitly in at least 4 calls) and the 183-day non-residency threshold (raised in at least 5 calls) — he needs this number before he can tell someone whether full personal tax-residency relocation is realistic, or whether they need a more conservative "keep UK residency, move the company" structure instead.

**D. Family and life situation**
- Whether a spouse could be a shareholder or the on-the-ground manager (repeatedly proposed — e.g. wives becoming shareholders/managers to satisfy "control and management" tests)
- Children, inheritance concerns, and estate planning intentions — this reliably surfaces the foundation/trust upsell (see Section 3)

**E. Business substance and activity type**
- What exactly the business does (trading, consulting, e-commerce, property, content/influencer income, professional services) — this determines free zone vs. mainland, IP-law 0% eligibility, or designated-zone eligibility
- Growth plans: number of future employees/visas needed, which determines free zone vs. mainland licensing

**F. Readiness / timeline signals**
- "When are you thinking of doing this?"
- Whether they have upcoming UAE travel already planned (used later as a natural deadline to structure the close around — "let's have this ready before your trip")

**Pattern for new reps:** don't pitch before you have (1) turnover, (2) current tax paid, (3) days spent in/out of the UK, and (4) family/succession context. These four numbers are what let Stallone tailor the specific structure and specific savings number in the pitch — pitching without them results in generic, less persuasive calls.

## 3. Presenting the Solution

Once discovery numbers are in hand, the pitch follows a fairly consistent sequence:

1. **Anchor on the tax mechanism, not the company itself.** He leads with the specific relief or rate that applies to their numbers — most often UAE Small Business Relief (0% corporation tax up to £600,000 / roughly 696,000 turnover, mentioned in at least 16 calls) or the 0%-up-to-$100k-then-9%-thereafter structure. This is stated as a concrete number tied to *their* turnover, not a generic "UAE has low tax" pitch.

2. **Reframe their current UK tax bill as the "before" picture.** Having gotten them to say their number out loud in discovery, he restates it back ("your current UK tax liability of £75,000...") immediately before presenting the UAE alternative, creating a direct before/after contrast.

3. **Introduce the structure in layers, from simple to sophisticated, escalating only as needed.** For straightforward cases this stops at a single free zone trading company. For higher-net-worth or more complex prospects, he builds up a **three-layer structure**: a UAE trading entity → a holding company → a foundation, explicitly framed as trading (operations) → holding (consolidation/dividend routing) → foundation (asset protection, anonymity, and succession/inheritance planning). He does not lead with the foundation for smaller prospects — it's introduced later, once trust and the base structure are agreed, and is explicitly pitched around inheritance tax and creditor protection, not just income tax.

4. **Explain the compliance mechanism up front, not as a rebuttal.** He proactively explains "control and management" (mentioned 18+ times) — i.e., that HMRC will look at whether the company is genuinely managed from the UAE — and the need for a local manager or genuine UAE presence. Framing this as a *feature* of a properly built structure (rather than waiting for the prospect to raise it as a risk) is a deliberate technique: he wants to be the one who introduces the compliance requirement, so it reads as thoroughness rather than a gap he's covering for.

5. **Quote logistics concretely.** Timelines are given as specific day-counts, always (7 working days for company setup, 5 for entry permit, 5 for residency visa, 10 for bank account — repeated near-verbatim across multiple calls), which makes the process feel operationally real rather than hand-wavy.

6. **Price last, and price as a total-cost breakdown, not a single number.** Setup fee, manager fee, and ongoing accounting/compliance fee are itemized separately (e.g. "$8,260 setup + $1,726 accounting = $10,000 total," or "£12,545 initial setup" broken into license, establishment card, visa, medical, and address costs). He frequently nets this against the tax saved in the same breath ("total cost of $17,000... potential tax savings of $13,000") so the fee is presented next to the payback, not in isolation.

**Pattern for new reps:** the order is Mechanism → Their Number Reframed → Structure (simple first, layer up only if the case warrants it) → Compliance Explanation → Timeline → Itemized Cost vs. Savings. Skipping straight to "here's what it costs" without first re-anchoring on their current tax pain consistently produces weaker calls in the transcripts.

## 4. Handling Hesitation / Objections Live

The technique, more than the specific answers (which live in the FAQ document), is consistent across calls:

**Validate first, then reframe with a bigger structural point.** When a prospect raises a risk (bank account safety, HMRC scrutiny, losing control of funds, a partner's reaction), Stallone doesn't argue the feeling away — he acknowledges it explicitly, then reframes using ownership/control language. Example pattern, seen repeatedly: a prospect worries about losing control of money or shares once a manager is appointed; Stallone's reframe is consistently some version of "the shareholding stays yours — the manager's name is only on the trade license, and you can prove ownership to anyone by showing the shareholder register." (Seen almost verbatim with Rafiq, and structurally identical with Reece and Karan.) The reframe is always about separating *legal control* (which the prospect keeps) from *operational visibility* (which is delegated) — this is his go-to device whenever "control" is the objection.

**Use "safety through relationships," not just paperwork, to answer risk objections.** When prospects worry about bank account safety, sanctions exposure, or being scammed by an agency, he doesn't just cite regulation — he personalizes the reassurance: "I'll handle it through one of my trusted contacts," or points to specific named banks he has relationships with (Emirates NBD, FAB, HSBC, Standard Chartered). The technique is to convert an abstract institutional-risk objection into a concrete, personally-guaranteed relationship, which is harder to argue with than a policy explanation would be.

**Reframe cost objections against the savings number, not against the fee itself.** When a prospect balks at price (or cites a cheaper competitor quote — this happens more than once, e.g. Dean), he doesn't defend the number in isolation. He (a) explains why cheap providers cost more later (hidden fees, exit fees, no ongoing support), and (b) restates the fee as a fraction of the tax saved, not as a standalone expense. He'll also proactively discount or restructure payment terms in real time when this comes up (see Section 5) rather than holding a hard line — the transcripts show him lowering an $17,000 quote to $15,000 for Marta, and splitting Gareth's payment into two tranches, both live on the call.

**Use "this isn't right for you yet" as a trust-building move.** In several calls (Marcus's cleaning business, Oliver's pre-revenue jewelry/supplement businesses), Stallone actively talks a prospect *out* of setting up immediately, explaining the setup cost isn't justified given their current revenue, and proposes revisiting later. This isn't passivity — it functions as a credibility play: prospects who see him decline easy revenue trust his advice more on the deals he does recommend, and several of these prospects are logged with an explicit future follow-up date rather than being dropped.

**Normalize the "I need to think about it / talk to my spouse or accountant" objection by scheduling the next step inside the objection.** Rather than resisting when someone wants to consult a spouse or accountant, he agrees readily but immediately attaches a concrete next action and a date to the "thinking" period (see Section 5) so it doesn't go cold.

## 5. Closing Techniques

Stallone's closes are notable for being **soft on pressure but hard on structure** — he rarely pushes for an immediate yes on the call itself, but he is very disciplined about never letting a call end without a specific, dated next action from both sides. Recurring close patterns:

- **A short confirmation window, not an immediate close.** The most explicit example: with Gareth, the next step was "Gareth to confirm readiness to proceed within 24 hours." This 24-hour (occasionally 48-hour, occasionally "within a week or two") confirmation window recurs across the dataset as the standard mechanism for converting interest into a decision without demanding one on the spot.

- **A modest, specific upfront payment to start.** Rather than asking for full payment or a vague "let's get started," he quotes a concrete partial figure to secure the engagement, then sequences the rest. With Gareth: "approximately 6,600 pounds upfront and 6,000 pounds in September for management fees" — split explicitly to "ease financial burden." With Reece: a £5,000 manager fee quote alongside separate visa costs (£900 for a 2-year visa, one visa "free for life" as a promo). He will actively restructure payment into two steps live on the call if a prospect signals financial hesitation (explicitly offered to Gareth: "a two-step solution to ease financial burden, allowing Gareth to pay manager fees after setup").

- **Manufactured urgency via real promotions with real deadlines**, not generic scarcity. The recurring device is a time-boxed offer — most often the "visa-free for life" promotion — tied to a specific cutoff: "if the license was submitted by the end of the day, [prospect] would have until the following Monday to make the payments," or "£900 'visa for life' promotion," or the IBSA December visa-free-setup promotion mentioned to Edward. Because these are logged as expiring on specific calendar dates tied to a real free-zone promotion, they function as legitimate urgency rather than a pressure tactic — this is a technique worth preserving carefully (only use real, dated promotions).

- **Booking the next call before ending this one.** Nearly every transcript's Next Steps section includes a mutually-scheduled follow-up ("Callan and Stallone: Schedule and hold a follow-up meeting on Tuesday," "Stallone and Oliver: Schedule a follow-up meeting at the end of August to establish the UAE company"). He treats "we'll be in touch" as a failure state — the default close is always a named day/week, not an open-ended "let me know."

- **Sending the quotation same-day, often "within the hour."** Multiple Next Steps bullets commit Stallone to sending a written quotation within an hour of the call ("Stallone: send quotation for company costs and manager costs within the next hour"). Speed of follow-through is itself part of the close — it keeps momentum from a warm call rather than letting it cool over days.

- **Anchoring the close to a real external deadline in the prospect's life**, when one exists — an upcoming UAE trip, a promo cutoff, a new financial year, a child returning to school. He books setup work to land just before these dates ("Oliver... plans to travel to the UAE in September... schedule a follow-up meeting at the end of August to establish the UAE company").

**Pattern for new reps:** every call should end with (1) a concrete next action for the prospect, (2) a concrete next action for you, (3) a specific date/window for the next contact, and — where the prospect is ready — a specific partial payment figure with an easy on-ramp (split payments, reduced first tranche) rather than a single large ask.

## 6. Follow-Up Cadence and Next-Step Patterns

Looking across all 103 meetings' Next Steps sections, a small set of commitment types repeats constantly:

- **Stallone almost always owes the next move.** The most common Stallone-side commitments are: send a written quotation/proposal by email (very frequent — "quotation" appears in 19+ meetings), send a link to his book on Amazon about UAE tax structuring (recurs across many unrelated prospects — a standing trust-building/authority asset he hands out generously, not just to hot leads), connect the prospect to a relevant specialist in his network (a UK tax advisor, an ex-HMRC compliance colleague, a VAT specialist named Adnan, a manager candidate), and schedule a specific follow-up meeting.

- **Prospects are given a homework item that keeps them engaged without asking for money yet** — e.g. "discuss the proposal with your wife," "review the quotation and prepare questions," "check with your current accountant whether X is included," "calculate your numbers and email me your decision." These are lightweight, low-friction actions that keep the prospect actively moving rather than passively "deciding."

- **A visible referral and repeat-business engine sits behind the 1:1 calls.** Stallone frequently mentions a formal referral program, offers commissions to intermediaries (lawyers, accountants, coaches) who send him clients, and books guest appearances on prospects' podcasts/platforms in exchange for exposure. Several calls are explicitly framed as trust-building for a longer relationship (offering after-sales support, ongoing accounting relationships, "VIP treatment") rather than a single transactional sale — this is consistent with the itemized separation of setup fee vs. ongoing annual fee seen in nearly every quote.

- **Workshops and seminars feed the pipeline.** A meaningful slice of the calls are explicitly logged as "Free Zero Tax Business Workshop" follow-ups — 1:1 calls booked directly off a free educational webinar/workshop. In these calls Stallone skips most of the "who am I" framing (the prospect already knows) and moves straight into discovery, suggesting the workshop funnel is meant to pre-qualify and pre-warm leads before the 1:1 ever happens.

- **When a deal isn't ready, it's parked with a date, not dropped.** Several Next Steps sections explicitly log "revisit in September," "reconnect once revenue grows," or "check back after the roll-up" — evidence of a disciplined long-cycle nurture habit rather than a one-and-done sales approach.

## 7. Example Scripts (in Stallone's observed voice)

**Opening a call (workshop follow-up / warm referral):**
> "Thanks for jumping on — I run Alliance Street Consultancy, we help UK business owners set up properly in the UAE and stay fully compliant with HMRC while doing it. Before I get into any of that, tell me a bit about what you've got going on at the moment — what's the business, roughly what's it turning over, and how much time do you actually spend in the UK each year?"

**Transitioning from discovery into the pitch:**
> "Okay, so based on what you've told me — turnover around [£X], and you're currently paying about [£Y] a year in UK tax — there's a relief available right now called Small Business Relief that means income up to £600,000 can sit at 0% corporation tax in the UAE. The way we'd usually build this for someone in your position is a trading company to run the business day to day, and depending on what you want long-term for the family, a holding structure on top of that. Let me walk you through how that actually works and what it would look like for your numbers specifically."

**Handling a control/trust objection ("but if someone else is the manager, don't I lose control of my company?"):**
> "Totally fair question, and it's the one everyone asks. Here's the thing — the manager's name is what shows up on the trade license, that's it. The shareholding is yours, full stop, and that's what proves ownership — you can show the share register to anyone, your partners, your bank, whoever, and it proves the company is yours. The manager is there to satisfy the control-and-management piece HMRC looks at. You're not handing over the business, you're delegating the paperwork."

**Handling a cost objection ("I got a cheaper quote elsewhere"):**
> "You probably did — there's a lot of that in this market. What I'd ask is what's actually included in that number, because a lot of the cheaper providers make it back on exit fees, extra charges for the annual return, or no support when the bank asks a question six months in. What I quoted you covers setup, the manager, and ongoing compliance, and against the [£X] a year you're currently losing to UK tax, it pays for itself inside the first year. Let's have a look at what's actually in their number versus what's in mine."

**Closing / booking next steps:**
> "Here's what I'd suggest — I'll get you a full written quotation over today, this afternoon if I can. Have a look at it, talk it through with [spouse/accountant], and let's regroup [Tuesday / in 24 hours] to see where you've landed. If you want to move on it, we can get the license moving with a first payment of around [£X] and sort the rest once the bank account's live — that keeps it manageable rather than one big number up front. Sound fair?"
