# FAQ & Objection Handling

## Legitimacy, Legality & HMRC Compliance

**Q: Is this legal? Isn't this just tax evasion?**
A: Stallone's consistent position is that this is legal tax *avoidance* (structuring), not tax *evasion*, provided the structure has real economic substance — a genuinely UAE-based, UAE-controlled and UAE-managed entity, not a shell used purely to redirect UK income. He points to documentation, arm's-length transfer pricing, and a locally based manager making real decisions as what separates a compliant structure from an illegal one. With Desmond (a UK property refurbisher), when he raised concerns about "tax evasion charges from HMRC," Stallone acknowledged the risk was real but said that with proper documentation and alignment between the UAE entity's activity and Desmond's actual business, the move could be done compliantly — and offered to loop in a UK tax advisor (often referred to as "Sanjeev" or his UK chartered accountant team) to validate the plan before proceeding. _(Raised in a large number of calls — one of the most common objections.)_

**Q: How do I actually prove "control and management" is in the UAE, so HMRC doesn't just tax the UAE company as if it were a UK company?**
A: Stallone explains this is the single most important compliance requirement. He typically recommends: (1) appointing a local manager based in the UAE who is the person signing contracts, invoices, and bank documents (not the UK-based owner); (2) regular travel to the UAE by the shareholder/owner to make and document strategic decisions there; (3) maintaining a genuine UAE office/address and, where relevant, staff; and (4) keeping paperwork (board minutes, invoices, contracts) that shows decisions were made in the UAE. He's told multiple clients (Tunde, Rafiq, Kwame, Minh) that the only document any government body sees is the trade license — showing the manager's name — while the client's shareholding proves ownership privately. He is candid that this doesn't need the owner to relocate permanently, but it does need genuine, demonstrable substance, not just paperwork.

**Q: What does the "local manager" actually do, and can I trust them with my money?**
A: The manager's role is primarily to sign contracts, invoices, and banking paperwork on behalf of the company to demonstrate UAE-based control — not to control the client's money. Stallone has repeatedly reassured clients (e.g., Reece/Dean, Gareth, Patrick) that despite the manager's name appearing on the trade license, the shareholder/owner retains full access to funds via online banking, a company debit card, and full visibility into the account. He typically charges roughly £5,000 for a dedicated manager, though for some cases he suggests using a trusted family member (a spouse, adult child, or business partner already living in or willing to relocate to the UAE) instead, to avoid that cost — as he suggested to Reece (using his partner Sophie) and to Deepak (using his son Rohit as a co-signatory).

**Q: Won't large, regular payments from my UK company to a UAE company raise red flags with HMRC?**
A: Stallone acknowledges this is a valid concern — Karan raised it explicitly, worried that big monthly transfers to a "tax-efficient" jurisdiction would attract scrutiny. His answer is that the payments need a legitimate commercial justification (a real service being provided — marketing, management consultancy, IP licensing, sourcing/procurement) charged at a fair, arm's-length rate, not an arbitrary "profit share." He's cautioned clients (e.g., Desmond) that inflating a management fee well beyond a normal commercial rate (he used 10% as a benchmark Desmond felt was close to the ceiling of a defensible, arm's-length rate) is a compliance risk, and has offered to have an ex-HMRC investigations colleague review structures for clients wanting extra assurance (e.g., Craig).

**Q: Do I need a nominee director, or is that itself risky/shady?**
A: No — when Tunde asked this directly, Stallone confirmed a nominee director is not required in his structures; the client remains the legal shareholder throughout.

**Q: Will my business partners/clients/university partners find out or be uncomfortable if ownership moves to a UAE entity?**
A: This came up with Rafiq (education recruitment agency, worried how his UK university partners would view a UAE-based recipient of commissions) and with Karan (worried about how a change in visible "ownership"/manager would look to a difficult business partner). Stallone's answer in both cases: the trade license only shows the manager's name publicly, while the client can privately show his actual shareholding to reassure partners or clients he still owns and controls the business — the change doesn't need to be broadcast, and in Rafiq's case Stallone also suggested a UK bank account linked to the UAE company as a practical convenience for UK counterparties used to paying a domestic account.

**Q: I got a much cheaper quote from another provider — why should I pay more?**
A: Stallone doesn't dismiss cheaper competitors outright but warns that low headline prices often exclude banking, visa, accounting, and compliance support, and can carry hidden "exit fees" or lack of ongoing HMRC-facing documentation support. With Edward (comparing to "MyStart UAE"), he pushed Edward to specifically check whether the cheaper provider's package included corporate tax registration and compliance services, noting that banking and tax management — not the initial license fee — are what matter for long-term success. With Dean, who'd received lower quotes from friends' providers, Stallone emphasized the value of a full-service, relationship-based offering (VIP airport pickup, medicals, hands-on account management) versus a bare-bones license sale, and encouraged Dean to speak to friends in Dubai about their actual experience with cheaper providers before deciding.

**Q: Is a "visa-free for life" promotion a sales gimmick?**
A: When Tunde questioned this, Stallone confirmed it was a genuine limited-time offer from the free zone authority (not his own gimmick) and called the free zone rep (Tanya) live on the call to verify the terms and deadline — illustrating his approach of proving offers are real rather than just asserting it. This promotion (visa fee waived for the life of the license, provided you complete setup within a promo window) recurs across many calls as a genuine cost-saver worth roughly £800–£900 every two years.

## Cost & Fees

**Q: How much does it cost to set up a company in the UAE?**
A: Costs vary by jurisdiction (Dubai mainland/free zone vs. Ras Al Khaimah/other Northern Emirates), number of shareholders/visas, and package inclusions, but typical figures across calls cluster around **£6,000–£8,500 / $8,000–$8,260** for a first-year all-inclusive setup (license, establishment card, one visa, medical/biometrics, virtual office, and Alliance Street's service fee). Examples: Kwame's total was around $7,800; Marta's Cyprus/UAE combined quote was $10,000 rising to ~$15,000–$17,000 with a manager; Elena's father's setup was quoted at around 19,000 AED including a 7,000 AED service fee; Dominic was quoted roughly £20,000 AED (~£4,000) for a Northern Emirates location vs. around £30,000 for Dubai. Stallone consistently breaks cost down into: government/license fee, establishment card, visa/medical costs, and his service fee (~$2,000 base per some quotes), and notes that Northern Emirates (e.g., Ras Al Khaimah/"Russell Kema"/RAK) licenses run cheaper than Dubai or Abu Dhabi.

**Q: What does it cost to keep the company running each year?**
A: Ongoing/renewal costs are generally lower than year one — commonly quoted around **£4,500–£5,000** for license renewal, plus accounting/bookkeeping (e.g., 500 AED/month mentioned to Elena; around $1,700/year mentioned to Marta), plus visa renewal every two years (~£800–£900, often free under the "visa-free for life" promotion), plus audit costs of roughly **3,000–4,000 AED (~£800)** depending on turnover. Marta's second-year cost, including a manager, was quoted at $12,500.

**Q: Is there a manager fee on top of setup costs, and can it be deferred?**
A: Yes — a dedicated local manager typically costs around **£5,000**. Stallone has shown flexibility here: with Gareth, he structured a "two-step" payment (an initial ~£6,600 to start setup, with the ~£6,000 manager fee payable later in September once the bank account was live) specifically to ease the client's cash-flow burden, and offered a reduced service fee ($1,000) to accommodate Gareth's situation. This shows he will negotiate/phase payments for clients who are otherwise a good fit but cash-constrained.

**Q: Is it worth the setup cost if my turnover/tax savings are small?**
A: Stallone says no, explicitly, in several calls — he turns away or discourages clients where the math doesn't work. With Marcus (small cleaning business), he said the UAE setup and management costs weren't worthwhile at Marcus's current profit levels, and suggested revisiting once Marcus scaled via roll-ups. With Ryan (an air-conditioning business already paying a well-optimized £23,000 corporation tax plus £60,000 CIS labour tax), the advisor (Egberth, one of Stallone's team) concluded a UAE structure costing £5,000–£6,000 wouldn't provide sufficient benefit and recommended against proceeding. With Oliver (pre-revenue supplement/jewelry startup), Stallone advised delaying setup because there was no current tax liability to offset the £6,000–£7,000 cost against, and with Jayesh's software signals business, Stallone noted a projected $20,000 revenue year wasn't enough to justify a UAE structure with $5,000+ setup costs. _(This is a useful pattern to note for objection handling: Stallone's credibility is partly built on turning away unprofitable deals rather than hard-selling everyone.)_

**Q: What's the actual tax saving I can expect?**
A: This is calculated case by case, but Stallone frequently frames it as "does the tax saving clearly outweigh setup + running costs, ideally by several multiples." Examples of savings quoted: Farhan (UK property portfolio) — reducing an annual liability from an original higher figure down toward ~£60,000 post-transition, or Desmond saving on his ~8,000/month company income; Ravi — Stallone estimated a foundation would return roughly 8x its £5,000 annual cost once the property started generating income; Graham (UK rental income) — estimated ~£16,000/year saved by achieving personal UAE tax residency; Callan — potentially reducing tax owed on £1–2 million of transferred profit using multiple entities and Small Business Relief. Stallone generally wants to see savings that are large relative to the ~£6,000–£10,000 setup and ~£5,000–£10,000/year running costs before recommending a full structure.

## Process & Logistics

**Q: How long does the whole setup process take?**
A: Stallone quotes a consistent multi-stage timeline across virtually every call: **company formation, 5–7 working days** (remote, from KYC documents) → **entry permit, ~5 working days** → **residency visa (medical/biometrics), 5–7 working days**, requiring the applicant to be physically present in the UAE for that window → **bank account opening, 7–10 working days** after the Emirates ID is issued. All-in, most clients are told to expect to be fully operational (company + visa + bank account) within about **30 days**, assuming no delays.

**Q: Do I need to travel to Dubai? Can this be done remotely?**
A: Company formation itself can be done fully remotely with passport/KYC documents. However, the **residency visa requires an in-person visit** for medical tests (blood test) and biometrics (fingerprint + retina scan) — typically a **5-working-day trip**. Stallone tells clients roughly 95% of applicants complete this within the standard 5-day window, with about a 5% chance of delay (usually medical-test related). Several clients (Kwame, Rohan, Elena's father, Dominic) planned specific trips around this requirement, and Stallone offers VIP arrival support (airport pickup) as part of the "relationship-based" service.

**Q: Once I have a UAE company, how much time do I need to spend there to keep it valid?**
A: For company/license validity alone, requirements are light — Farhan was told a Golden Visa property investment needs only a visit "once every 10 years" to remain valid, and several clients were advised that visiting the UAE roughly **once every six months** is enough to demonstrate ongoing control and management for HMRC purposes (used with Karan, Desmond, Elena's father). This is a separate, higher bar from *personal UAE tax residency*, which requires 90 days/year physically present (see Tax Residency section below).

**Q: What paperwork/information do you need from me to get started?**
A: Standard KYC: passport copy, date of birth, mother's maiden name, proposed company name, and business activity description. For more complex cross-border cases (e.g., Deepak's India-to-UAE holding structure), additional attestation (MOFA legalization) of foreign entity documents is required and can take about a month.

**Q: Can I choose my own accountant / handle banking myself instead of using your team?**
A: Yes — Edward specifically wanted to select his own accountant and deal directly with banks, and Stallone accommodated this while still recommending his own banking relationships (e.g., Musharaka Bank) as the more efficient path based on his personal contacts there.

## Banking

**Q: Will I have trouble opening a UAE bank account?**
A: It depends heavily on the license type and whether the applicant has an Emirates ID. "High-risk" categories — general trading licenses, gold/precious metals trading, and businesses without a resident Emirates ID — are frequently rejected by banks over money-laundering compliance concerns; Stallone explained this directly to Marlon, whose Vio application was declined because the bank doesn't onboard general trading licenses and he lacked an Emirates ID. His standard fix is to obtain the Emirates ID first (e.g., via Shams), then reapply, or to use alternative European e-money institutions that don't require UAE residency.

**Q: Which banks do you recommend, and why?**
A: Stallone rotates recommendations based on the client's needs: **Wio** (UAE Central Bank-licensed, multi-currency, low fees, fast digital onboarding), **EBRI** (a Banco Santander subsidiary using Barclays as custodian), **Musharaka Bank** (efficient service via Stallone's personal relationships), **Mashreq** and **RAK Bank** (for trade licenses), **Emirates NBD** (for larger fixed deposits/Golden Visa investment routes), and **3S Money** (a Luxembourg-based payment institution offering IBANs, positioned as an alternative to a UAE bank with different documentation requirements — it remains subject to standard Common Reporting Standard obligations like any other reporting financial institution). For higher-net-worth or international needs he's also mentioned **Ebury** (Santander-backed, 30+ currencies, low documentation) and named FAB, HSBC, and Standard Chartered as banks he has strong relationships with.

**Q: Will HMRC see my UAE bank account and balances automatically?**
A: This came up implicitly across several calls. Stallone's answer is nuanced: the UAE participates in Common Reporting Standard (CRS) automatic information exchange, so UAE financial institutions - including custodian/EMI providers like 3S Money - can report account information to HMRC for UK tax residents in the normal course of CRS reporting; a company account is a separate legal entity from a personal account, but that doesn't itself change the underlying reporting obligations. He's warned clients directly, though (e.g., Dominic, Dr. Whitfield), that once someone is no longer UK tax resident, they should be careful about *using UK personal bank accounts* to receive UAE company funds, since that can itself trigger UK tax exposure — better to spend via a UAE company debit card usable worldwide (including in the UK), rather than transferring money into a personal UK account.

**Q: Can I get a debit card I can use back home?**
A: Yes — this is a recurring reassurance. Stallone confirms company debit cards can be used for spending and cash withdrawals in the UK (and worldwide), and in some cases arranges for a physical card to be delivered directly to the client in the UK, so they don't need to be present in the UAE to access funds day-to-day.

**Q: What ongoing balance/fees does a UAE business account require?**
A: Typically either an average balance requirement (commonly ~50,000 AED average balance mentioned to Marlon and Beatrice) or a flat monthly fee (~200 AED/month) if the balance requirement isn't met.

## Personal UK Tax Residency & Living Abroad

**Q: How many days can I spend in the UK before I'm still considered UK tax resident?**
A: Under the UK's Statutory Residence Test (SRT), Stallone generally advises clients to stay under **183 days** in the UK per tax year, and ideally under **90 days** if they want to claim non-residence cleanly and avoid ties like family/home/work day thresholds complicating the test. He's repeatedly flagged that even under 183 days, factors like a UK family home, property, or spending 30+ working days in the UK can trip the SRT's "sufficient ties" test (explained in detail to Reece/Dean and Nolan). Nolan was told he could work up to 30 days in the UK without violating non-resident status. Several clients (Callum, Dean, Minh) were advised the 90 days abroad doesn't need to be consecutive and can be split across trips.

**Q: How do I become a UAE tax resident (not just have a UAE company)?**
A: Requirements consistently cited: spend a minimum of **90 days** in the UAE within a 12-month period (not necessarily consecutive), have a **UAE residential address** (rented or owned), and have a company that has been operational for roughly **6 months with regular income**, in order to qualify for a UAE Tax Residency Certificate. This certificate is what's needed to invoke the UK-UAE Double Tax Treaty and to formally evidence non-UK residence to HMRC.

**Q: Do I need to formally notify HMRC that I've left the UK?**
A: Yes — Stallone consistently advises filing **Form P85** with HMRC upon departure to formally register non-residence (mentioned to Dr. Whitfield, Nolan, Minh, Callum, and others).

**Q: If I leave and come back within a few years, will HMRC tax me retroactively?**
A: Stallone warns that under UK's temporary non-residence rules, if you return to the UK within **5 years** of leaving, certain income/gains realized while "non-resident" (e.g., large dividends) can become taxable again retroactively. He's flagged this specifically to Callum and Lachlan as a reason to think of UAE relocation as a genuine multi-year commitment (3–5 years minimum), not a short-term dodge.

**Q: Is there an "exit tax" if I leave the UK with property/business assets?**
A: This came up with Reece/Dean, who expressed concern after hearing about a proposed ~20% exit tax on assets under consideration by HMRC at the time. Stallone confirmed this was a real policy under discussion but not yet in force, and recommended factoring the risk into timing decisions rather than dismissing it.

**Q: Can my spouse/family stay in the UK while I run a UAE business, without risking UK tax residency for the company?**
A: Yes, this is workable but requires care. Several structures involve one spouse living/managing in the UAE (satisfying control-and-management) while the other remains in the UK drawing an income (e.g., dividends) from a UK holding company that receives funds from the UAE entity. Stallone cautions, though (as with Lachlan, whose children attend UK school), that strong UK family ties — especially children in UK schools — can themselves anchor UK tax residency regardless of where the shareholder claims to spend time, so this needs case-by-case SRT analysis.

**Q: Can I keep a UK property and still claim non-residence?**
A: Generally yes if managed carefully — Nolan was told renting a UK property for occasional personal visits shouldn't be a problem, but Stallone separately warned Nolan against maintaining *other* UK ties (e.g., using the property as an office) while simultaneously claiming to have left the UK, as inconsistent ties undermine the non-residence claim.

## Structures: Holding Companies, Foundations & Trusts

**Q: What's the difference between a UAE foundation, a trust, and a holding company — which do I need?**
A: Stallone uses these somewhat interchangeably in casual conversation but distinguishes them when it matters: a **holding company** owns shares in operating entities and is useful for tax-efficient dividend flows and share-for-share exchanges (e.g., Preston, Lachlan, Craig); a **foundation** (a UAE common-law foundation) is a separate legal person with no individual "owner" — it has directors and beneficiaries governed by bylaws, making it well suited to asset protection, anonymity, and succession/inheritance planning (Ravi, Patrick, Farhan, Callan's 3-layer structure); a **trust** serves a similar succession-planning purpose and can hold property or investments without personal tax liability, sometimes used as an additional layer on top of a foundation or company (Gareth, Karan, Elena). Foundations are generally recommended over simple holding companies when clients want inheritance/UK-IHT protection or stronger separation from personal ownership.

**Q: Why would I want a foundation instead of just owning the company myself?**
A: Two main reasons recur: (1) **UK inheritance tax (40%)** avoidance — Ravi and Farhan were both advised that assets held via a UAE foundation, rather than personally or via a simple company, can pass to children without triggering the standard 40% UK inheritance tax; (2) **privacy/protection from creditors or scrutiny** — Craig wanted "anonymity and protection from creditors" while remaining HMRC-compliant, and Callan's proposed 3-layer structure (trading entities → holding company → foundation) was explicitly framed as being for "ultimate protection and anonymity."

**Q: What does a foundation cost, and is it worth it just for future/uncertain income?**
A: Roughly **£5,000/year** to establish and maintain, per the quote given to Ravi. Stallone was candid with Ravi that a foundation "would not solve his current tax issues" (since the property wasn't yet generating income) but would pay for itself roughly 8x over once rental income began — i.e., the recommendation was to set it up ahead of the income event, not as an immediate tax fix.

**Q: Can a UAE holding company own my existing UK limited company without triggering UK capital gains tax?**
A: Yes, via a **share-for-share exchange** — moving shares of the UK company into a new UAE (or UK) holding company structure without a taxable disposal event. This was proposed to Zaid (selling his UK tech company), Lachlan (avoiding CGT on a UK software company potentially worth a substantial sum), Preston, and Craig. Stallone flags that while this avoids CGT at the point of exchange, ongoing UK tax residency of the individual shareholder is a separate question that still needs solving (e.g., Lachlan's children being in UK school kept him UK tax resident regardless of the share structure).

## Small Business Relief & UAE Corporate Tax Specifics

**Q: How much can my UAE company earn before paying any tax?**
A: Under the UAE's Small Business Relief, revenue up to a threshold is taxed at **0%** for a limited multi-year window (most often cited as through 2026 or for the "next 3 years" depending on when the call took place). The exact revenue ceiling is quoted inconsistently across calls — most commonly **£600,000** (Callan, Kwame, Reggie), but also **$800,000** (Jayesh's later meeting), **€800,000** (Elena), and **€696,000** (Marta) — reflecting currency/FX framing more than a hard rule change; clients should be told the exact current threshold and expiry date at proposal stage rather than relying on any single figure. Above the relief threshold (or once it lapses), profit is taxed at **9%**, though the **first $100,000 of net profit is always tax-free** regardless of the Small Business Relief status.

**Q: Are there capital gains or dividend taxes in the UAE?**
A: No — Stallone confirms repeatedly (to Callum, Lachlan, Warren/Arjun, and others) that the UAE has **no capital gains tax and no dividend tax**, which he positions as a major long-term advantage for clients planning to eventually sell a business or extract accumulated profit.

**Q: What is the "IP law" / 0% corporation tax option I've heard about?**
A: This refers to a UAE tax incentive for qualifying intellectual property income (software, trademarks, etc.), which can achieve **0% corporation tax** — but only if specific conditions are met, including genuine local development activity and UAE-based employees (explained to Nathan/Owen, and pursued in more depth with Lachlan's software company and Craig's HVAC trademark IP idea). Stallone contrasts this with the simpler, lower-compliance option of a standard 9% free-zone company, and generally recommends IP structuring only for clients with meaningful IP value and the resources to meet the substance requirements.

**Q: If my UK company invoices heavily to a UAE entity, will that reduce my UK corporation tax bill?**
A: Yes, in principle, via legitimate intercompany service charges (marketing, management, IP licensing) — but Stallone stresses these charges must reflect real services at a commercially reasonable (arm's-length) rate. He used a current UK borrowing-rate benchmark of **7–10%** as a guideline for interest on intercompany loans with Callan, and cautioned Desmond that a management fee much above a normal commercial rate (he referenced 10% as the ceiling Desmond was comfortable with) risks scrutiny.

## Golden Visa & Investment Residency

**Q: What do I need to qualify for a UAE Golden Visa?**
A: The standard route cited repeatedly is a minimum **AED 2,000,000 investment** in UAE real estate (or, in Elif's case, via a bank deposit/investment route) held for a minimum of **3 years**, plus (in the banking route) maintaining roughly an additional 500,000 AED average balance. This grants a 10-year renewable residency and allows sponsoring family members. Golden Visa via property was discussed with Grant (2M AED property purchase) and via bank investment with Elif.

**Q: Can I get a Golden Visa as an influencer/content creator, not through property investment?**
A: There is a discretionary route via the Ministry/Media Council for individuals who can demonstrate value to the UAE (e.g., high social following), but Stallone was explicit with Reece/Dean that these rules are "subject to change" and less predictable than the standard investment route — approval can take **2–6 weeks** and requires building a strong supporting proposal. He recommended clarifying whether the client's real goal was tax savings or physical residency before choosing this path over a more standard company/employment visa route.

**Q: What's the minimum time I need to spend in the UAE to keep a Golden Visa valid?**
A: Farhan was told there's no strict minimum stay requirement for Golden Visa holders — visiting roughly once every 10 years is sufficient to maintain the visa (though this is separate from what's required to maintain UAE *tax residency*, which does require the 90-day rule).

## Industry-Specific Questions

### Property & Real Estate

**Q: I own UK rental property personally — can moving it into a UAE structure reduce my tax?**
A: Stallone's usual answer distinguishes two separate levers: (1) transferring UK property into a UK limited company (a separate exercise with its own UK tax/SDLT considerations, which he generally refers out to a UK accountant rather than advising on directly — flagged as risky by Graham, whose colleagues had "faced significant tax bills" attempting it) versus (2) the property owner personally relocating to become non-UK tax resident, which can allow rental profit to be drawn more tax-efficiently once outside the UK's income tax net. With Graham (£4M portfolio, £120,000 profit), Stallone recommended the personal relocation route over a risky corporate transfer. With Farhan and Graham, moving property income through a UAE company (with the owner or spouse achieving UAE tax residency) was estimated to save roughly £16,000–£20,000+/year depending on portfolio size.

**Q: I do property development/refurbishment — can my UK company pay a UAE entity for "management" to cut my tax bill?**
A: Yes, structurally, but Stallone is careful to size the management/service fee at a defensible commercial rate (see Desmond's case — capped around a 10% fee, treated as close to the ceiling of what's commercially defensible) and stresses the UAE entity must have genuine local management to support the arrangement.

### E-commerce

**Q: Can my e-commerce brand's profits sit in a UAE entity while I keep selling into the UK?**
A: Yes — Stallone's typical structure has a UAE entity act as the wholesale/purchasing hub (consolidating supplier purchases) while a UK entity continues local retail sales, using transfer pricing to shift margin to the UAE side. Discussed at length with Callum (a UK decorative lighting import brand) and Oliver (supplement/jewelry e-commerce), with the caveat (per Oliver) that pre-revenue or early-stage e-commerce businesses often shouldn't set up in the UAE yet — better to wait until there's actual tax liability to offset the setup cost against.

### Healthcare & Regulated Professions

**Q: I'm a doctor/psychologist/health professional — can I just set up an online consultancy in the UAE?**
A: This is one of the more constrained categories. With Dr. Whitfield (UK psychiatrist relocated to UAE), Stallone flagged that UAE-regulated healthcare practice (e.g., psychology) generally requires practicing **under a licensed clinic** rather than independently, unlike the UK's more flexible remote-practice norms; independent practice requires passing UAE licensing exams and can require significant setup capital (~50,000 AED plus office rent). For Imran's dermatology-referral platform, Stallone clarified the correct structure was a **software/marketing facilitation company** (not a clinic license), since the business connects patients and doctors and processes payments/marketing rather than delivering medical care directly.

### Trading, Crypto & Financial Services

**Q: Can I trade crypto/forex through a UAE company tax-free?**
A: Broadly yes for the entity's profits (subject to the same 0%/9% thresholds), but Stallone flags two practical friction points repeatedly: (1) **opening a crypto-friendly bank account is genuinely harder** — Rohan's attempt with Kingdom Bank was unsuccessful due to lack of UAE documentation, and crypto-related activity is treated by UAE banks as a higher-scrutiny category, so Grant's license and marketing materials needed to accurately describe his actual business rather than being generically worded, since banks cross-check the stated activity against what they find in due diligence; (2) regulatory/licensing nuance for investment advisory or brokerage activity — Nolan was told his license activity description needs to precisely match investment-advisory work to satisfy bank compliance checks, and Patrick's sugar-trading/financial-brokerage business needed a specific **commercial brokerage license**, not a generic trading license.

### Influencers & Content Creators

**Q: As a content creator/influencer, can I move my brand-deal income into a UAE structure while still working with UK brands?**
A: Yes, in principle — discussed at length with Dean and Reece, where Stallone proposed a UAE entity receiving the majority of brand-deal income (with the influencer taking only a smaller portion for UK living expenses), while stressing the influencer must genuinely separate themselves from day-to-day control of the income-generating entity to satisfy HMRC. He noted this works best paired with an actual relocation plan (spouse or the influencer themselves spending real time in the UAE), not as a pure paperwork exercise.

### Consultancy & Coaching

**Q: I run an online coaching/consultancy business with UK clients — does my UAE company need a UK presence too?**
A: Often a two-entity structure works best: a UAE entity holds the company/manages finances, while a UK-facing entity (or simply UK-compatible payment processing, e.g., Stripe with a UK bank leg) handles client-facing payments so UK clients see no disruption. This was the structure proposed to Kwame (online coaching) and discussed for Nolan (stock market investing education) and Nathan/Owen's AI health app coaching angle.

### Education & Recruitment

**Q: My business collects commissions/fees from UK institutions — can those be redirected to a UAE entity to save tax?**
A: This is more complicated when the UK counterparty (e.g., a university, in Rafiq's case) is reluctant to pay a foreign entity directly. Stallone's approach was to set up a UK bank account attached to the UAE company, so the UK payer could continue paying into a UK account it already recognized, while the underlying company remains UAE-owned/controlled — and to have the UK company act as a "sourcing agent" invoiced by the UAE entity for services, shifting margin without disrupting the payment relationship.

### Import/Export & Trading

**Q: I trade physical goods (food, metals, gold) internationally — what license do I need?**
A: Depends heavily on the goods' risk profile. Stallone explained to Warrick that substantial international food trading (>$200,000 net profit) could use a **designated zone license** for 0% corporation tax, while **gold/precious metals trading** is treated as high-risk and requires either a **mainland license or a DMCC free zone license**, plus a security agency contract for physical transport, and generally requires the trader to demonstrate **5+ years of experience** in gold/jewelry trading before banks will onboard the account.

## VAT-Specific Questions

**Q: Can moving my UK service business to a UAE entity reduce my VAT bill?**
A: Sometimes, but it requires care about where the service is actually delivered/consumed. Stallone explained to Reggie (UK service-accommodation business) that UK-delivered local services still incur UK VAT regardless of who owns the entity, but that services *genuinely exported* from the UAE to the UK (e.g., outsourced back-office work) would not carry UK VAT. He explicitly flagged this as an area requiring specialist VAT input (referencing a colleague, "Adnan") before confirming any specific structure, and Dr. Whitfield was advised that re-registering her practice correctly could bring VAT down from an inflated effective rate to the standard 20%, separate from any UAE structuring.

**Q: My company is VAT-registered in the UK with high turnover — will a UAE entity get me out of VAT?**
A: Not automatically — with Rafiq (education recruitment, ~£1M turnover, VAT-registered), Stallone's structure focused on reducing net UK profitability (and therefore corporation tax) by routing marketing/supplier costs through a UAE entity, while being clear that VAT exposure specifically required separate analysis with his UK tax team, since VAT treatment depends on where the service is deemed supplied, not simply on company ownership.

## When a UAE Setup Is NOT Worth It (Stallone's Own Disqualifiers)

**Q: When does Stallone actually advise against setting up a UAE structure?**
A: This recurs enough to be worth calling out as a category in itself, since it's a credibility-building pattern rather than a hard-sell approach. Stallone (or his team, e.g., Egberth) has advised against proceeding when: the client's current UK tax burden is already well-optimized relative to setup/running costs (Ryan, air-conditioning business — £83,000 combined tax was deemed already efficient, and a ~£5,000–£6,000 UAE structure wouldn't move the needle); the business is pre-revenue or revenue is too uncertain to justify locking in setup costs (Oliver's supplement/jewelry startups); projected turnover is too small (Marcus's cleaning business at current scale; Jayesh's $20,000 projected revenue year). In these cases the standard advice is to revisit once the business scales, or to consider UAE **investment opportunities** (real estate, deposits) rather than a full operating company structure in the meantime.
