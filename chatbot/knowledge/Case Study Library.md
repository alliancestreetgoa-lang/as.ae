# Case Study Library

## Purpose and Selection Method

This library distills 20 named case studies from the full set of 103 real client call transcripts behind Fast Track to Zero Tax / Alliance Street Consultancy. Where the Master Knowledge Base organizes the same material by topic (tax rules, structures, costs, compliance) and the FAQ document organizes it by objection type, this library organizes it by **client story** — the kind of narrative a salesperson (or the GPT) can reach for and say "here's how a similar client's situation played out."

Cases were chosen to span:
- **Industry** — property/real estate, e-commerce, SaaS/tech, professional consultancy, healthcare, trades, crypto/trading, education, influencer/content, and recruitment.
- **Deal size** — from marginal/borderline cases not worth pursuing yet, to mid-size owner-operators, up to a multi-million-pound fund structure.
- **Outcome** — clients who proceeded and paid deposits, clients still weighing the decision, and — deliberately included because they are instructive — clients Stallone (or a colleague on his team) advised **not** to proceed with a UAE structure at that time.

Client names throughout this library have been replaced with fictional first names to protect real client identities; situations, figures, and outcomes are otherwise drawn directly from the call summaries. Where a name was ambiguous in the source transcript (e.g., a client referred to by two different first names across a call), that is noted using the fictional name assigned here.

## Case Study 1: Property Developer Weighing a Three-Layer Structure — Callan

**Client profile:** UK-based property developer running a project management/development business, evaluating a full UAE holding structure.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Callan wanted to reduce UK tax exposure on his property development income and was open to a fairly sophisticated structure rather than a single simple entity.

**Objections/concerns raised:** Callan wanted to run the proposal past a new UK accountant he was in the process of interviewing before committing.

**Structure recommended:** Stallone proposed a three-layer structure: a UAE trading entity to run the project management business, a holding company above it, and a foundation on top for ultimate asset protection and anonymity. He also flagged UAE Small Business Relief — 0% corporation tax on the first £600,000 of income — as directly applicable to Callan's situation.

**How objections were handled:** Stallone offered to recommend UK accounting firms who are "open-minded" about offshore structuring, recognizing that Callan's decision would partly hinge on getting sign-off (or at least non-opposition) from his own UK adviser.

**Outcome / next steps:** Callan agreed the structure made sense in principle and said he'd think it over during a trip to Mallorca and after meeting the new accountant. A follow-up call was scheduled for the following Tuesday.

**Key takeaway:** Multi-layer structures (trading + holding + foundation) land well with clients who are already used to complexity (developers, multi-entity operators) — but expect a "let me check with my accountant" pause before they commit, and pre-empt it by offering a same-side accountant referral rather than letting the client's existing adviser become a blocker.

## Case Study 2: Property Portfolio Landlord Seeking UAE Residency — Farhan

**Client profile:** UK property investor (Farhan) with a mixed commercial/residential portfolio worth around £1 million, generating roughly £120,000 a year before expenses; currently paying a modest sum in UK tax but in the process of moving the portfolio into a limited company, after which tax would rise to an estimated £60,000/year.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Farhan was mid-transfer of his properties into a UK Ltd company and wanted to know whether relocating to Dubai could reduce the tax hit once that structure was in place, and how to protect the properties for his children long-term.

**Objections/concerns raised:** None major — Farhan's main questions were practical: minimum Golden Visa stay requirements and how the UK Statutory Residence Test (SRT) would apply to him, since he already spends about 6 months of the year outside the UK.

**Structure recommended:** Stallone laid out the SRT path to non-UK tax residency (183+ days outside the UK, up to 90 days back in the UK with no more than 30 workdays), which would let Farhan withdraw money from his new Ltd company without UK dividend tax. For the long-term estate question, he recommended transferring the bulk of Farhan's properties (both UK and the Dubai properties Farhan already owns, two rented and three not yet transferred) into a UAE foundation to reduce personal income tax exposure and shield against UK inheritance tax.

**How objections were handled:** N/A — Farhan was already believed to be close to the 183-day non-resident threshold, so this was more information-gathering than objection handling. Stallone confirmed that a Golden Visa only requires a UAE visit once every 10 years to stay valid, removing a logistics worry.

**Outcome / next steps:** Stallone sent Farhan the HMRC SRT details and arranged to meet him in person in Dubai (Business Bay) on his next visit, around end of September, to progress the foundation setup for the properties.

**Key takeaway:** For landlords already spending significant time abroad, the sale isn't "should I leave the UK" — it's "how do I formalize what I'm already doing" (SRT residency) plus a forward-looking estate-planning layer (foundation for inheritance tax). Anchor the pitch on documentation and protection, not persuasion.

## Case Study 3: Dubai Property Owner and the Foundation-vs-Company Decision — Ravi

**Client profile:** UK-based investor (Ravi) who already owns Dubai real estate worth approximately 4.5 million AED.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Ravi wanted to know how to structure his existing Dubai property holdings to avoid future UK income tax on rental income and, more importantly, UK inheritance tax (40%) when the properties eventually pass to his children.

**Objections/concerns raised:** Cost sensitivity — a foundation costs roughly £5,000/year to establish and maintain, and Ravi questioned whether that was worth it given the properties aren't currently generating income. He also raised the general "bureaucratic hassle" of dealing with UAE administration, and implicitly the question of Sharia law implications on succession (Stallone agreed to send a written breakdown covering this).

**Structure recommended:** An English common-law foundation domiciled in the UAE to hold the properties. Stallone was direct that the foundation wouldn't solve any *current* tax problem (there's no income yet) but would pay for itself roughly 8x over once the properties are producing rental income (projected around 350,000 AED net income by 2029–2030), and would remove the 40% UK inheritance tax exposure entirely. He also compared the foundation option against simply holding the properties in a standard Dubai-registered company.

**How objections were handled:** Reframed the £5,000 annual cost against the 40% inheritance tax bill it would avoid and the future rental income it would shelter (8x ROI framing), rather than arguing the near-term numbers.

**Outcome / next steps:** Stallone agreed to email a detailed pros/cons breakdown of the foundation, including the Sharia law question. Ravi said he'd discuss it with his children and give an answer within one to two weeks.

**Key takeaway:** When a structure's benefit is mostly forward-looking (future income shelter, avoided inheritance tax) rather than an immediate saving, quantify it as a multiple of the annual cost ("8x your £5,000") — pure percentage-tax-rate framing doesn't land as well when there's no current income to compare it to.

## Case Study 4: E-Commerce Brand Scaling Toward a UAE Holding Structure — Callum, a UK decorative lighting import brand

**Client profile:** Callum runs a UK decorative lighting import brand, a UK e-commerce business importing and selling high-end decorative lighting from the UK, Europe, India, and China. Turnover grew from £274,000 to a targeted £400,000 in the current year.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Callum was already paying UK corporation tax and wanted a scalable structure ahead of further growth (and an eventual exit in 7–10 years), without disrupting his existing UK sales operation.

**Objections/concerns raised:** Callum was cautious about timing — he wanted to model 18–24 months of growth projections before committing, and had practical questions about whether Shopify's payment gateway would work with a UAE bank account.

**Structure recommended:** A UAE-based holding company linked to the UK trading brand, using transfer pricing to consolidate purchasing under the UAE entity while keeping UK-facing sales in the UK Ltd co — letting profit accumulate tax-free in the UAE while UK operations stay lean. Stallone flagged the UAE's 0% corporate tax treatment for wholesale traders in designated zones and discussed a starter budget of £4,000–6,000. He also raised UAE tax residency (90 days/year) as a personal-level option for Callum, alongside a possible future move to Italy under its 7% flat-tax regime for new residents.

**How objections were handled:** Rather than pushing for an immediate decision, Stallone reframed the ask as market research: encouraged Callum to visit the UAE this year (not wait until next year) to see the opportunity firsthand, and offered to connect him with a UAE-savvy Shopify/logistics contact to de-risk the payment-gateway question before Callum commits capital.

**Outcome / next steps:** Callum agreed to review Stallone's book on digital-nomad tax strategy, research the Shopify/UAE banking question, and plan a Dubai trip for February/March to explore the market and meet in person. No structure was signed yet.

**Key takeaway:** Growing e-commerce brands respond well to a "holding company + transfer pricing" pitch that lets them keep UK sales operations intact while shifting margin offshore — but they need the operational plumbing (payment gateways, logistics, supplier relationships) de-risked before committing, not just the tax case.

## Case Study 5: AI Health & Beauty App and the UAE IP Law Route — Nathan & Owen

**Client profile:** Two-person team building an AI-powered booking CRM for the health and beauty industry: Nathan (building the product, dev team based in Dubai) and Owen (UK-based sales specialist in data centers/cloud, planned as co-shareholder and eventual head of UK sales).

**Starting situation / pain point:** Nathan's app was nearly complete (targeting a March/April launch) and the pair wanted to structure IP ownership and sales revenue in a tax-efficient way from day one, with Nathan initially staying in the UK and moving to the UAE once the business could support him.

**Objections/concerns raised:** None explicit yet — this was an early-stage exploratory call, with the main open question being whether one of the partners could be formally appointed as the UAE company's manager to satisfy control-and-management requirements.

**Structure recommended:** Stallone presented two options: (1) the UAE's IP law route offering full 0% corporation tax, contingent on meeting substance requirements (local development activity, UAE-based employees); or (2) a simpler standard free-zone company at the flat 9% rate. He also suggested a separate structure for Owen specifically, since Owen intended to remain UK-resident and would need his own plan (e.g., dividends via a UK holding company) to reduce his personal UK tax exposure.

**How objections were handled:** N/A at this stage — Stallone committed to sending full written detail on both options (IP law conditions/costs, and the basic free-zone structure) so the partners could compare before deciding.

**Outcome / next steps:** Stallone to email the IP law requirements and the basic free-zone company details (one visa, two shareholders, Small Business Relief). Nathan and Owen to review and confirm whether one partner can act as UAE manager, and to consider Alliance Street's management services if not.

**Key takeaway:** For IP-heavy tech/SaaS businesses, the UAE's IP law (true 0% rate) is a strong differentiator over the standard 9% free-zone company — but it comes with real substance conditions (local development, local staff), so set expectations early that "0% tax" isn't automatic just because the company is UAE-incorporated.

## Case Study 6: Accounting Consultancy Owner Closes on a Time-Limited Visa Promo — Tunde

**Client profile:** Tunde runs an accounting business in the UK and, as an accountant himself, was already tax-literate going into the call.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Tunde was paying high UK taxes on his accounting practice and had been exploring a living trust as an alternative before this call turned toward a UAE company structure instead.

**Objections/concerns raised:** Price anchoring — Tunde had been quoted £3,000–7,000 by other UK-based agencies for "company setup" and pushed back until Stallone clarified those quotes didn't include a residency visa or bank account, unlike Alliance Street's £6,500 first-year all-in price. He also questioned the legitimacy of a "visa-for-life" promotional offer tied to a Free Zone deadline, suspecting a sales gimmick.

**Structure recommended:** A UAE trading entity where the UAE side manages/controls a UK marketing outsourcing relationship — Tunde's UK company would keep taking most of the income, with regular UAE travel and management decisions there to prove control-and-management to HMRC. Commercial terms: ~£6,500 first-year cost (license, entry permit, residency visa, medicals, fingerprinting, bank account, office address), £4,500 license renewal thereafter, visa renewal every two years, and Small Business Relief giving 0% tax up to £600,000 for the first three years, 9% above that.

**How objections were handled:** On price, Stallone itemized exactly what the £6,500 covered that the cheaper competitor quotes did not. On the promo skepticism, Stallone called the Free Zone representative (Tanya) live on the call to confirm the visa-for-life offer was genuine and time-boxed (submit by end of day, pay by the following Monday) — turning the "is this a gimmick" objection into urgency.

**Outcome / next steps:** Tunde agreed to proceed, provided the required registration details (company name, passport, DOB, mother's maiden name), and Stallone's admin team took over the formalities.

**Key takeaway:** When a prospect is comparing your all-in price against a stripped-down competitor quote, don't argue the number — itemize what's actually included (visa, bank account, compliance) that the cheap quote leaves out. And a genuine, verifiable time-limited promo (confirmed live, not just claimed) is one of the most effective closes in the whole dataset.

## Case Study 7: Education Recruitment Agency Fighting a VAT and Payment-Routing Problem — Rafiq

**Client profile:** Rafiq runs a UK-based student recruitment agency (placing students with universities) with turnover around £1 million, VAT-registered.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Rafiq's core problem wasn't really tax rate — it was cash flow and VAT: his UK university partners were reluctant to pay commissions into a UAE-registered entity, and he was absorbing costs (video annotation/data services) his contracts said partners should be covering. He was paying 20% VAT and wanted it reduced.

**Objections/concerns raised:** Rafiq was skeptical the structure would actually work in practice — his university partners might simply refuse to pay a UAE company, and he was also uneasy about a UAE entity being run by a different named manager while he retained beneficial ownership, worrying how that would look to his existing business partners.

**Structure recommended:** Stallone proposed the UK company act as a sourcing/marketing agent that bills a new UAE entity only for its real costs (salaries, expenses, marketing — not a marked-up fee), while the UAE entity — with a separate manager on the trade license — bills the UK universities directly. As a genuine cost-recharge arrangement rather than a profit-marked-up fee, this would not create a VAT liability in either jurisdiction. To support genuine control-and-management substance, Stallone recommended a genuine UAE office and employee, with the manager's name (not Rafiq's) on the public trade license, while Rafiq could privately show clients/partners his shareholding as proof of ownership.

**How objections were handled:** On the "will universities even pay a UAE entity" concern, Stallone's answer was practical, not theoretical: have Rafiq go back and simply ask his university partners whether they'd accept payment to a UAE company with a UK bank account attached, rather than assuming the answer. On the ownership-optics concern, Stallone reassured him the trade license listing a different manager doesn't affect his real ownership, which he can prove privately to partners.

**Outcome / next steps:** Rafiq agreed to go back to his university partners with the UK-bank-account framing, and Stallone committed to consulting his own UK tax team on the VAT angle specifically. No structure signed yet — genuinely still deciding, pending partner feedback.

**Key takeaway:** Not every client's real blocker is UK tax rate — for agency/commission businesses, the practical question of "will my counterparties even pay this new entity" can matter more than the tax math, and it's worth having the client test that assumption directly rather than structuring around a hypothetical objection.

## Case Study 8: UK Psychiatrist Already Living in the UAE — Dr. Whitfield

**Client profile:** A UK-qualified psychiatrist ("Dr. Whitfield") who has already relocated to the UAE (renting an apartment, living there for several months) but still operates through a UK limited company with turnover around £120,000.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Dr. Whitfield is personally already a non-UK tax resident by virtue of the 183-day rule, but the UK company itself remains UK tax resident, so the structure hadn't caught up with the personal relocation — meaning UK tax and VAT were still being paid unnecessarily.

**Objections/concerns raised:** Practical/regulatory concerns dominated: client data protection issues around delivering UK client sessions remotely from the UAE, and the fact that UAE psychology/psychiatry practice rules require working under a licensed clinic (remote-only assessment isn't permitted), unlike the more flexible UK locum model Dr. Whitfield was used to.

**Structure recommended:** Stallone laid out a menu of options rather than a single fix: (1) deregister the UK company for VAT and restructure it properly as a health-professional entity (bringing VAT down from 20%); (2) use a "no-tax code" arrangement so Dr. Whitfield could draw a salary from the UK business as an employee without UK tax, given non-residency; (3) route dividends into a UAE entity to receive them tax-free rather than paying UK dividend tax; or (4) the more involved option of a UAE parent company owning the UK Ltd as a subsidiary, with UK corporation tax paid first and remaining profit passed up as tax-free dividends. For actually practicing in the UAE, Stallone flagged the ~50,000 AED mainland licensing cost and the requirement to pass UAE professional exams (scheduled for January) before UAE regulators would approve an independent or clinic-based practice.

**How objections were handled:** Rather than overselling immediate UAE practice, Stallone sequenced the advice: get the exams done first, keep working remotely for UK clients under the existing (now-optimized) UK company in the interim, and revisit UAE practice licensing once qualified. He also brought in a UK tax advisor for a joint follow-up call to validate the plan.

**Outcome / next steps:** Stallone to loop in his UK tax team and Dr. Whitfield's own UK tax advisor for a follow-up meeting, and to have his team research UAE psychology practice requirements in more depth.

**Key takeaway:** Regulated professionals (doctors, psychologists, lawyers, accountants) often have a *personal* residency situation well ahead of their *corporate* structure — the fix isn't always "move everything to the UAE," it's often "align the company with where you already, legally, live," plus separately navigating local professional licensing if they want to practice in-market.

## Case Study 10: Training-Course Business Owner with Crypto Gains — Marta

**Client profile:** Marta runs in-person training courses across Norway, Denmark, Sweden and other European countries, and separately made roughly $60,000 in cryptocurrency trading profit the previous year.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Marta wanted to know how to structure both the training business and her crypto gains tax-efficiently, and was weighing the UAE against other jurisdictions given her business is EU-centric, not UAE-facing.

**Objections/concerns raised:** Cost-consciousness about ongoing management/director fees, and a preference to keep crypto profits reinvested rather than distributed as dividends (she only wanted the option to take dividends "if necessary").

**Structure recommended:** Rather than defaulting to a UAE company, Stallone actually presented a **Cyprus** company as the better fit given her European client base — establishment fee around $8,300 plus around $1,700/year accounting, or roughly $17,000 all-in with a manager, against an estimated $13,000 in tax savings, with the added benefit that dividends taken outside the EU aren't taxed. **Outcome / next steps:** Stallone to review and try to further reduce the director/manager cost and send an updated written proposal; Marta to model the numbers herself before responding. Not yet signed — genuinely still deciding.

**Key takeaway:** Not every prospect belongs in a UAE structure — when a client's revenue base is EU-centric, a Cyprus (or other EU-adjacent) entity can be the more honest recommendation, and offering it (rather than force-fitting UAE) builds the kind of trust that closes the next deal.

## Case Study 13: Multi-Million-Pound Real Estate Fund Structure — Arjun & Warren

**Client profile:** One of the larger deals in this set — Arjun and his partner Warren (corporate finance/entrepreneurship background) planned to pool several million pounds from UK investors into a Dubai/Abu Dhabi off-plan real estate investment vehicle, targeting a multi-year hold with exits via secondary sale or trading.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Warren and Arjun had spent a year doing market research and believed there was strong investor appetite, particularly among UK nationals already established in the UAE, but wanted a lighter-weight structure than a full regulated investment fund, which would take ~8 months and significant cost to set up.

**Objections/concerns raised:** How to give larger investors real security/control without the cost and delay of a fully ring-fenced regulated fund; how share transfers involving property would work (requiring UAE Land Department approval); and general due diligence on Stallone's track record versus other providers they'd worked with.

**Structure recommended:** Stallone proposed a General Partnership Limited by Shares (GPLP) structure via the DIFC — a lighter alternative to a full fund, supporting up to 50 shareholders/investors as preference or ordinary shareholders, startable immediately (versus 8 months for a full fund). He modeled a scenario of 10 million AED deployed across 5 assets over 18 months: a 50% return on equity would generate 2.5 million AED gross profit, with the first $100,000 tax-exempt and the remainder taxed at 9% (net ~2.15 million AED after tax) — no capital gains tax in the UAE. He also flagged foundations as a complementary structure for inheritance tax planning for individual investors, and a UK holding company for tax-efficient dividend repatriation under the UAE-UK double tax treaty (15% rate). Setup costs quoted: ~$20,000 for license/incorporation plus office space (6,000 AED/month to 15,000 AED/year), and an estimated $25,000/year total running cost, with a UAE-based manager required since both Arjun and Warren live in the UK.

**How objections were handled:** On investor security without a full fund's cost, Stallone proposed joint signing authority for withdrawals as the middle ground between investor protection and operational flexibility. On market timing (this call took place against a backdrop of regional tension), Stallone reassured them that foreign interest in UAE real estate — from the UK, Netherlands, and Southeast Asia — remained strong regardless.

**Outcome / next steps:** Stallone to send a full proposal and cost breakdown; Arjun and Warren to send comparable fund/structure templates for reference and follow up in a few weeks after making progress on the investor side.

**Key takeaway:** For fund-style, multi-investor deals, a lighter DIFC GPLP structure can capture most of the benefit of a full regulated fund (pooled capital, share classes, tax efficiency) at a fraction of the 8-month setup time and cost — a valuable alternative to pitch when a large deal's real blocker is speed-to-market, not size.

## Case Study 14: Construction/Land-Sourcing Consultancy and the HMRC Scrutiny Objection — Karan

**Client profile:** Karan runs a UK-based operation involving land sourcing and project coordination services (construction-adjacent), moving roughly £6,000/month (up to ~£100,000/year) between entities, and was also dealing with a problematic business partner and missing funds.

**Starting situation / pain point:** Karan wanted to route payments for land-sourcing and coordination services through a lower-cost UAE entity, and separately was considering having a friend invest additional capital into the structure.

**Objections/concerns raised:** This case has the most explicit HMRC-scrutiny objection in the dataset — Karan was directly worried that large, regular monthly payments to a company in a tax-efficient jurisdiction would draw HMRC investigation, and separately worried about maintaining a clean separation between his own funds and his friend's invested capital.

**Structure recommended:** Stallone proposed a management consultancy license in the UAE covering land-sourcing and project-coordination services, with a UK holding company added above the UAE operational entity (the operating entity and bank account were set up first, with the holding company layered on afterward) for standard dividend-routing and ownership structuring. For asset protection and to address Karan's privacy/scrutiny concerns, Stallone discussed a complementary trust structure (a Cyprus trust was also floated) holding Karan's interests, plus separate debit cards for the holding/free-zone entities. He also required Karan to visit the UAE roughly every six months to maintain genuine, documented management decision-making there, consistent with the control-and-management test.

**How objections were handled:** Rather than dismissing the HMRC scrutiny concern, Stallone worked through the actual compliance logic with Karan — profit-sharing/intercompany service rules, the need for a genuine and defensible service being rendered (not just a paper arrangement), and the six-monthly visit requirement as the concrete evidence trail HMRC would look for. This transparency, rather than reassurance alone, is what moved Karan from "worried this looks dodgy" to "this could work."

**Outcome / next steps:** Karan decided to consult his own accountant before proceeding, with both sides agreeing to revisit sooner if Karan's accountant is comfortable with the approach.

**Key takeaway:** When a client's objection is specifically "will this draw regulatory scrutiny," the right response is to walk through the actual substance requirements (genuine services, defensible pricing, real management presence) rather than simply reassuring them — clients who understand *why* a structure is defensible are more likely to proceed, and more likely to actually maintain compliance once they do.

## Case Study 15: High-Earning Director Restructures Around HMRC Debt — Gareth (Proceeded)
